Abstract

Wi-Fi devices can effectively be used as passive radar systems that sense what happens in the surroundings and can even discern human activity. We propose, for the first time, a principled architecture which employs Variational Auto-Encoders for estimating a latent distribution responsible for generating the data, and Evidential Deep Learning for its ability to sense out-of-distribution activities. We verify that the fused data processed by different antennas of the same Wi-Fi receiver results in increased accuracy of human activity recognition compared with the most recent benchmarks, while still being informative when facing out-of-distribution samples and enabling semantic interpretation of latent variables in terms of physical phenomena. The results of this paper are a first contribution toward the ultimate goal of providing a flexible, semantic characterisation of black-swan events, \ieevents for which we have limited to no training data.

I Introduction

Wi-Fi devices can effectively be used as passive radar systems that sense what happens in the surroundings and can even discern human activity [1].

In this paper, we propose – for the first time – a principled analysis of a recently captured dataset of human activities sensed by commercial Wi-Fi devices, using Variational Auto-Encoders (VAEs) [2] for identifying generative relationships with a latent distribution which we use as a compressed view of the original signal. Specifically, we focus (Section II-A) on the scenario illustrated in [3], where a commercial Wi-Fi device performs human activity recognition (HAR) through the analysis of the channel state information (CSI) – a measurement of the wireless channel’s properties – detecting the specific activities performed by a target person inside a room. We evaluate different methods to fuse the data collected simultaneously by different antennas of the same Wi-Fi receiver to improve the overall HAR performance.

VAEs (Section II-B) are generative models which can be trained to learn causal relationships (\cfFig. 2) between a latent distribution – a multi-variate normal distribution in our case – and the training dataset. Differently from standard auto-encoder architectures already employed in analogous HAR tasks [4], where the latent space does not have a specific semantic meaning, the VAEs’ assumption that data observations are caused by a latent distribution provides us with a principled method for identifying reasons for the perturbation of the perceived signal.

An uncertainty-aware classifier, notably using the Evidential Deep Learning (EDL) loss function [5] (Section II-C), then utilises the latent distribution of a data sample to identify the actual activity and estimate the aleatory (or aleatoric) and epistemic uncertainties [6], hence giving our system the ability to identify out-of-distribution (OoD) samples (\iepreviously unseen activities).

Our experimental results (Section IV) – which build upon the Delayed-Fusing VAE-based architecture we describe in Section III-A – support our main hypotheses (Section III-B), \iethat VAEs provide a concise yet informative characterisation of the activities perceived in the Wi-Fi signal. In particular, results outperform existing state-of-the-art benchmarks on the same dataset by fusing the sensing of multiple antennas. Not only does our Delayed-Fusing architecture outperforms existing state-of-the-art benchmarks, but it also appears to be informative when facing OoD samples (Section V-A) and provides a semantic interpretation of the VAE latent distributions through an inherently interpretable model (Section V-B).

As we comment in the conclusions (Section VI), these results are a first contribution toward the goal of providing a flexible, semantic characterisation of black-swan events, \ieevents for which there is limited to no training data.

II Background

II-A Motivating Scenario

In this paper, we consider an application for HAR in indoor environments using Wi-Fi sensing techniques. Specifically, we focus on the scenario illustrated in [3], where a commercial Wi-Fi receiver is used to sense the environment through the analysis of the CSI and detect specific activities performed by an unspecified person inside a room.

In wireless communications, the CSI is an estimation of the wireless channel’s properties (see Fig. 1), computed by the receiver for every incoming Wi-Fi frame. The CSI is a critical element in orthogonal frequency-division multiplexing (OFDM) communication systems (including Wi-Fi) because it allows equalising frequency-selective distortions on wide-band communication channels by simply comparing symbols received in the frames’ preamble against a known reference signal [7]. Since such distortions are directly related to the multipath effect caused by the physical environment, CSI analysis is essential for many Wi-Fi sensing applications [8]. Indeed, the electromagnetic interaction of the wireless signals with the surrounding environment is captured by the CSI in the form of idiosyncratic interference patterns that depend on the room geometry, the furniture layout, and even the presence and the movements of human bodies. In other words, the CSI can be interpreted as an electromagnetic fingerprint of the environment, and Wi-Fi receivers can be considered under appropriate assumptions as passive radars.

In this work, we rely on a CSI dataset recently published for HAR [3]. The testbed consists of two Asus RT-AX86U routers, each of which has four antennas and has multiple-input multiple-output (MIMO) capabilities. During the experiments, one router generates dummy Wi-Fi traffic at a constant rate of 150 frames per second using the injection feature of AX-CSI [9], while the other router (also called monitor) uses the same software tool to do the sensing, \ieto collect the CSI from the received test Wi-Fi frames. We consider one single scenario from the dataset in which a person performs different activities in an indoor space of approximately \qty45m^2. For every activity, the monitor collects \qty80s of CSI data.

Figure 1 illustrates a snippet of the data captured by one single antenna, \vizthe magnitude of the CSI while a person is walking. As the person walks around the room, the effect of the environment on the signal changes due to the varying scattering on the human body. The result is captured in a sort of spectrogram that highlights how the relative intensity of the signal changes over time and frequency. The fundamental assumption of CSI-based HAR is that it is possible to trace these variations back to the human activity that caused them. Currently, state-of-the-art HAR systems work by deriving some physically-related quantity from the CSI that is then used to train a deep learning classification system, like in [10, 11, 12].

In this paper, on the contrary, we evaluate the possibility of using a principled data-driven approach to perform HAR. The idea of using data-driven approaches on radio data is not new per se [13], and an autoencoder-based HAR system has already been proposed in [4]. However, we find at least three points that separate our work from the current state of the art. First, our work builds upon CSI measurements made with commercial Wi-Fi systems, while [4] employs custom hardware integrated with a software-defined radio (SDR) platform. Second, by using an EDL loss function our approach can handle OoD samples. Finally, the causal assumptions behind the VAE-based architecture allow us to (post-hoc) explain the variables in the latent space in terms of physical quantities.

III Methodology and Hypotheses

III-A Architectures

Despite more than a decade of research on Wi-Fi sensing, there is still no one-fits-all solution to perform HAR using the CSI. In this work, we investigate and compare the performance of several modular architectures that we now present in detail.111Our dataset and code are available at https://zenodo.org/record/7983057 and at https://github.com/marcocominelli/csi-vae/tree/fusion2023.

The first set of architectures, called No-Fused-x𝑥xitalic_x and illustrated in Fig. 3, includes a VAE (VAE-Ax𝑥xitalic_x) with a latent bivariate normal distribution trained over the data coming from a single antenna of the Wi-Fi monitor – \egthe first antenna in Fig. 3 – and a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) for classification. The structure of the VAE is reported in Table I. Our monitor contains four antennas, hence we have four architectures trained separately, one for each antenna: No-Fused-1, No-Fused-2, No-Fused-3, and No-Fused-4.

As of today, most of the research on Wi-Fi sensing is still based on a single-antenna CSI extraction system [15]. While many works showed that this kind of data is informative enough for some HAR applications (\eg[16, 17]), modern Wi-Fi systems usually have more than one antenna available. Therefore, it is worth investigating whether there are any advantages in considering the multiple CSI data streams to improve Wi-Fi sensing performance.

The second architecture, called Early-Fusing and illustrated in Fig. 4, includes a VAE (VAE-F) with a latent bivariate normal distribution trained over the data coming from the four antennas of the monitor, and a MLP for classification. We also experimented on a variation (Early-Fusing-3D) of this architecture, where the VAE considers a latent tri-variate normal distribution (VAE-F-3D) and then a MLP for classification which takes as input the value of the latent space.

The third architecture instead, called Delayed-Fusing and illustrated in Fig. 5, includes the four VAEs trained on single antennas – the same used in No-Fused-1, No-Fused-2, No-Fused-3, No-Fused-4 – whose latent spaces are given as input to a single MLP for classification.

It is necessary to clarify how we feed the CSI into the VAEs. In general, the CSI is a complex vector representing the frequency response of the wireless channel (\cfSection II-A). However, in this work, we discard any information about the phase and we only consider the magnitude of the CSI for simplicity. At this point, a sequence of CSI can be interpreted as a spectrogram, exactly as reported in Fig. 1, which we normalize in amplitude with respect to the highest value measured in the entire dataset. Instead of feeding the entire spectrogram to the VAEs, we use a sliding window of \qty3s to select the input samples for the VAE. VAE-A1, VAE-A2, VAE-A3, and VAE-A4 receive in input the CSI data from their corresponding antennas. For VAE-F instead, we stack the CSI of the four antennas along a third dimension, in a way that can be analogously interpreted as four separate channels of a single image. It is important to notice that this is not the best solution for processing the CSI; on the contrary, we are deliberately removing some of the information. However, we leave experimentation with different data structures and manipulation techniques as future work.

III-B Experimental Hypotheses

We now formulate the hypotheses we will use later while presenting the experimental results (\cfSection IV).

We assume the target person can only perform a subset of the possible activities in the dataset in [3]. Specifically, we consider the following five activities: walk, run, jump, sit, and empty room. We choose them because i) they are the most basic types of activity conceivable, which have also been considered in related work on CSI-based HAR (\cf[10, 11]), and ii) we can easily compare the results against the benchmark performance reported in [3] over the same five activities.

Our main hypothesis is that the VAEs in the proposed architectures should provide a concise yet informative characterisation of the activities as perceived by the Wi-Fi monitor. The validity of this hypothesis can be assessed by qualitatively estimating the clustering performance of each VAE. We stress the fact that each VAE operates on the raw data (like the CSI shown in Fig. 1) without any notion of the semantics around the input data. When presenting the results in Section IV, we will compare the different architectures by measuring the classification accuracy over the MLP.

We expect the overall accuracy of the Delayed-Fusing architecture to be not inferior to the one obtained with the Early-Fusing framework, or with any of the single-antenna architectures No-Fused-1, No-Fused-2, No-Fused-3, and No-Fused-4. Since the four antennas of the monitor are spaced by more than half wavelength, the received signals are generally deemed uncorrelated due to the multipath effects. Hence, there should not be a real advantage in using a single Early-Fusing architecture. Instead, from an engineering perspective, it is arguably easier to employ four antenna-specific VAEs rather than trying to extract information from a tensor with four orthogonal components.

Trade-offs will be necessary, but we believe that Delayed-Fusing is a solution more appealing than VAE-F. V Post-Hoc Analysis and Discussions

The Delayed-Fusing architecture not only outperforms existing state-of-the-art benchmarks (see previous section), but it is informative for OoD samples, or black-swan events (Section V-A). Moreover, we show that we can provide a semantic interpretation of the VAE latent distributions by using an inherently interpretable model [18] (Section V-B).

V-A Dealing with out-of-distribution samples

EDL’s loss functions (Section II-C) can be used to handle epistemic and aleatory uncertainty. In particular, the intuition at the root of Eq. 5 is that ambiguous samples should lead to a uniform distribution, a special case of the Dirichlet distribution with all the parameters equal to 1.

We simulated this case by considering an activity – squatting – never used by any model trained so far, whether in training the VAEs or the MLPs: due to space constraints, we consider the Delayed-Fusing architecture only which has been trained to be more responsive to OoD (\cfSection IV-B) despite the risk of lower accuracy. Figure 8 depicts the distribution of the logarithm of the Dirichlet distribution parameters (pseudo-counts of evidence for each class) outputted by the MLP with the EDL loss function. When we consider in-distribution samples from the five target activities (Fig. 8(a)), the resulting distribution has an overall higher Dirichlet strength – that is, a higher number of pseudo-counts – than when considering OoD samples (Fig. 8(b)). This means that the proposed architecture effectively handles the uncertainty of the estimation, as we recall that the lower the Dirichlet strength, the closer the output is to the uniform distribution.

In future work, we will expand this preliminary investigation considering more articulated loss functions [6], including adversarial training with automatically-generated [19] or explicit [20] OoD samples, and more explainable models including probabilistic circuits, recently expanded for dealing with beta distributions [21] – a special case of the Dirichlet distribution.

V-B Physical interpretation of the VAEs’ latent space

To provide an interpretation of the VAEs’ latent spaces, let us consider the Delayed-Fusing architecture where the classification is provided by a decision tree instead of an MLP with EDL loss function. Figure 9 illustrates the resulting decision tree, which has an overall test accuracy of 0.910.910.910.91 despite being specifically designed to have at most three levels; Fig. 10 shows the confusion matrix.

By inspecting the resulting decision tree (Fig. 9), we can build the following working hypotheses concerning the physics captured by the various latent variables. Z1subscript𝑍1Z_{1}italic_Z start_POSTSUBSCRIPT 1 end_POSTSUBSCRIPT of the first antenna appears to be particularly sensitive to movements of an individual in the room: the first decision node, indeed, considers the expected value of this variable, and the leaves to the left are all belonging to the classes Empty or Sit. Z2subscript𝑍2Z_{2}italic_Z start_POSTSUBSCRIPT 2 end_POSTSUBSCRIPT of the second antenna seems to capture the presence of obstacles to the signal in the environment. Antenna 3 appears to have embedded the concept of speed, with its ability – combining its Z1subscript𝑍1Z_{1}italic_Z start_POSTSUBSCRIPT 1 end_POSTSUBSCRIPT and Z2subscript𝑍2Z_{2}italic_Z start_POSTSUBSCRIPT 2 end_POSTSUBSCRIPT – to distinguish between Run, Walk, and Jump.

This post-hoc analysis also suggests that not all the human activity considered in this dataset should be treated equally. Walking or running appear to be atomic while jumping, with different speeds of movement in the space, could be decomposed into a repeating sequence of – perhaps – squatting, walking, sitting. To this end, as part of future work, we plan to study HAR combining atomic actions using neuro-symbolic complex event processing systems, \eg[22, 23].

VI Conclusion

We propose, for the first time, a principled architecture which employs VAEs for identifying causal relationships within a latent distribution and a dataset of human activities measured by passive Wi-Fi radars, and Evidential Deep Learning for its ability to discern out-of-distribution activities. Not only does our proposed architecture (Section III-A) outperforms existing state-of-the-art benchmarks (Section IV), but it can also detect OoD samples (Section V-A). Moreover, we can provide a semantic interpretation of the VAE latent distributions by using an interpretable model (Section V-B).

The results of this paper are a first contribution toward the goal of a flexible, semantic characterisation of black-swan events, \ieevents for which we have limited to no training data. As part of future work, indeed, we aim at employing more sophisticated methodologies for estimating epistemic and aleatory uncertainties, \eg[19], and for reasoning about them, \eg[21], building upon also recent advancements in neuro-symbolic learning and reasoning, \eg[22, 23]. Future challenges include studying the ability of the proposed architecture to generalise to different people performing other activities in various environments [3], as well as different applications that make use of radar technology, \eg[24].
