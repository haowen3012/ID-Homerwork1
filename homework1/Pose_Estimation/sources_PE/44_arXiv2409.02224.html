Abstract

Estimating touch contact and pressure in egocentric vision is a central task for downstream applications in Augmented Reality, Virtual Reality, as well as many robotic applications, because it provides precise physical insights into hand-object interaction and object manipulation. However, existing contact pressure datasets lack egocentric views and hand poses, which are essential for accurate estimation during in-situ operation, both for AR/VR interaction and robotic manipulation.

In this paper, we introduce EgoPressure, a novel dataset of touch contact and pressure interaction from an egocentric perspective, complemented with hand pose meshes and fine-grained pressure intensities for each contact. The hand poses in our dataset are optimized using our proposed multi-view sequence-based method that processes footage from our capture rig of 8 accurately calibrated RGBD cameras. EgoPressure¬†comprises 5.0 hours of touch contact and pressure interaction from 21 participants captured by a moving egocentric camera and 7 stationary Kinect cameras, which provided RGB images and depth maps at 30 Hz. In addition, we provide baselines for estimating pressure with different modalities, which will enable future developments and benchmarking on EgoPressure. Overall, we demonstrate that pressure and hand poses are complementary, which supports our intention to better facilitate the physical understanding of hand-object interactions in AR/VR and robotics research. 1 Introduction

Having a sense of touch contact and pressure during hand-object interaction is crucial for a large variety of tasks in Augmented Reality (AR), Virtual Reality (VR), and robotic manipulation. In particular, estimating these physical properties from an egocentric perspective is a central enabler to support real world tasks in the physical space. In AR/VR environments, providing information about touch contact and pressure to the systems allows for more precise control and feedback. For example, when users play a virtual piano on a table, the sound can change based on the pressure applied to the keys, providing a more delicate and continuous feedback experience that current AR/VR systems lack. The sense of pressure is also precious to enable robots to better and more accurately replicate human manipulation. For instance, determining the precise amount of pressure needed to grasp objects is a considerable challenge for robots due to the lack of their limited manipulation ability.

To obtain pressure during contact, previous approaches have equipped gloves and robots with pressure sensors for continuous measurements while manipulating objects. While reliable, this added instrumentation makes interaction cumbersome and can impact users‚Äô behavior, as components obstruct their tactile sense of touch. In contrast, camera-based estimation can operate without requiring such added instrumentation, especially since vision sensors are commonly integrated in today‚Äôs head-worn displays as well as robots, often in multiple quantities and complementary modalities. Despite this promise, contact and pressure estimation from egocentric cameras alone requires datasets and annotations that are substantially rare at this point. A notable and pioneering exception is PressureVision¬†[1], a dataset of touch images and contact pressure values from a static vantage point. EgoPressure¬†aims to build on PressureVision‚Äôs effort to allow researchers to develop methods for egocentric platforms.

In this paper, we propose a novel dataset for contact and pressure estimation: EgoPressure. Our corpus comprises high-quality hand pose and pressure data from an egocentric view as well as from 7 other (static) vantage points as in Figure¬†1. Our capture platform integrates 8 synchronized Azure Kinect cameras for RGB footage and depth maps as well as a Sensel touch sensor for precise contact and pressure data. From the synchronized and accurately calibrated RGBD cameras, our capture system recovers 3D hand poses and meshes. In sum, EgoPressure¬†contains recordings from 21 participants, 64 sequences, and average 420 frames each, totaling 5.0 hours. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first bare-handed egocentric pressure dataset with accurate pose and mesh annotations.

EgoPressure¬†extends existing datasets on touch pressure in two central directions. 1)¬†Our dataset allows developing methods for estimating pressure from movable cameras (e.g., head-mounted systems or otherwise body-worn cameras). Static top-down perspectives are insufficient for accurate pressure estimates, and EgoPressure‚Äôs constantly moving sensor now provides realistic footage for this scenario. 2)¬†EgoPressure¬†includes accurate 3D hand poses, which are crucial for HMD-based systems. We present an optimization method to annotate hand poses from the multiple cameras in our setup, which accurately optimizes mesh-level representations to infer contact more precisely. Moreover, our precise mesh-based hand pose annotation with vertical displacement allows for reasoning about accurate manipulation as the pressure could be inversely projected to the hand meshes from the Sensel touch sensor.

Our study shows that hand poses and contact pressure are complementary. Recent HMDs (e.g., HoloLens‚Äâ2 and Quest‚Äâ3) provide modalities such as RGB, depth, and hand poses. These hand poses are used to control input in AR/VR. Augmenting this with pressure information can significantly enhance the degrees of freedom for interaction, while simultaneously improving hand pose estimations. Furthermore, we introduce a novel benchmark for estimating contact pressure from hand poses and egocentric RGB images, facilitating future research on egocentric pressure detection and egocentric interaction.

2 Related Work

Our work is related to hand-object pose estimation, contact estimation and pressure sensing.

Vision-based hand-object pose estimation

Hand tracking has been a long-standing challenge in computer vision, with applications in robotics¬†[12, 13], human-computer interaction¬†[14, 15, 16], and medicine¬†[17, 18, 19]. Over the past decade, significant progress has been made, largely due to advancements in deep learning techniques¬†[20, 21] and the collection of relevant datasets¬†[22, 23, 24]. While egocentric hand tracking for gesture recognition and direct input has advanced to the point of integration into modern commercial devices such as AR and VR headsets¬†[14, 15], understanding hand interactions with external objects remains an active area of research¬†[25, 5, 6, 26]. Datasets gathered to aid machine understanding of such hand-object interactions rely on additional instrumentation of the users‚Äô hands¬†[25], motion capture systems with hand-attached markers¬†[4, 5], or multi-view camera rigs¬†[6, 7, 8, 9, 10] to capture accurate ground-truth poses of users‚Äô hands under the higher degree of occlusion caused by the object.

Hand-object contact estimation

In addition to object-relative hand pose, prior work has aimed to estimate contact points between the users‚Äô hands and external objects¬†[4, 5]. Research has shown that when used as input proxies, real-world physical objects can improve input control and provide haptic feedback¬†[27]. For interactive research purposes, external tracking systems and wearable sensors such as acoustic sensors¬†[28] and inertial measurement units were used to estimate contact¬†[29, 30, 31, 32, 33, 34]. Additionally, vision-based techniques have been developed that use fiducial markers¬†[35], active illumination for shadow creation¬†[36, 37], vibration detection¬†[38], or depth sensing¬†[39, 40, 41, 42, 43, 44, 45, 46]. Prior work has also aimed to estimate detailed contact maps using passive cameras without additional instrumentation on the user‚Äôs hand or surface, enabling remote sensing and integration into head-mounted devices like AR and VR headsets¬†[47, 48]. These contact maps are based on the intersection of tracked hand and object meshes¬†[4, 5, 6, 8, 49], requiring sub-millimeter accuracy‚Äîa challenging task for complex gestures due to soft tissue dynamics. To address this, Brahmbhatt et al.¬†[3] used thermal imaging to obtain accurate contact maps. Additionally, prior efforts have utilized simulations to obtain more granular labels about contacting tissue¬†[50, 51, 52].

Hand pressure estimation

Researchers have aimed to estimate users‚Äô forces to better understand human actions, forming a basis for modeling and controlling intelligent agents¬†[53, 54]. Instead of focusing solely on contact, prior work has estimated the pressure forces applied during hand interactions, which is crucial for robotic grasping tasks¬†[55] and can provide an additional control dimension for input¬†[56]. To estimate pressure from monocular images, visual cues such as fingernail alterations¬†[57, 58] or object deformations¬†[57] during press events have been used. Changes in object trajectory and interaction forces¬†[59, 60, 61] also offer insights but are ineffective with static objects like tables and walls. Accurate pressure labels for training usually require instrumenting the user‚Äôs hands with gloves¬†[62, 63, 64] or the surface with force sensors¬†[61, 1, 65], ideally flexible or conforming to various shapes¬†[66, 67, 68]. However, this can alter the visual appearance and tactile features of the hands and surface, affecting interaction and limiting generalization to bare hands and uninstrumented surfaces. Grady et al.¬†[1, 2] collected two datasets with ground-truth pressure maps using a Sensel Morph¬†[69] pressure sensor to train a neural network for estimating contact regions on surfaces from single RGB images. However, their method relies on an external static camera and good visibility of the corresponding fingertips.

With EgoPressure, we aim to bridge this gap by offering a dataset that includes egocentric views, utilizing head-mounted cameras to better understand human interactions from this perspective. Our dataset also captures accurate hand poses and meshes from multiple camera views without the use of markers. To the best of our knowledge, we provide the first dataset containing egocentric and multi-view RGB-D images of a bare hand interacting with a surface, along with synchronized pressure data, hand poses, and meshes (see Table¬†1).

3 EgoPressure Dataset

EgoPressure comprises 4.3M RGB-D frames (2560‚Äâ√ó\times√ó‚Äâ1440 for static camera, 1920‚Äâ√ó\times√ó‚Äâ1080 for egocentric camera) capturing interactions of both left and right hands with a touch and pressure-sensitive planar surface. The dataset features 21 participants performing 31 distinct gestures, such as touch, drag, pinch, and press, with each hand. It includes a total of 5.0 hours of hand gesture footage comprised of synchronized RGB-D frames from seven calibrated static cameras and one head-mounted camera, along with ground-truth pressure maps from the pressure-sensitive surface captured at a frame rate of 30‚Äâfps. We used four different surface textures for the data capture rig, which also includes a green wall to facilitate synthetic background augmentation. Additionally, we provide high-fidelity hand pose and mesh data for the hands during interactions based on our proposed annotation method (see Section¬†4), as well as the tracked pose of the head-mounted camera. With EgoPressure, we aim to offer a substantial dataset for egocentric hand pose and pressure estimation during interactions with rigid surfaces, thereby advancing machine understanding of human interaction with their surroundings through the fundamental modality of touch.

3.1 Data capture setup

To capture accurate ground-truth labels for hand pose and pressure from egocentric views, we constructed a data capture rig that integrates a pressure-sensitive touchpad (Sensel Morph¬†[69]) for touch and pressure sensing, along with seven static and one head-mounted RGB-D camera (Azure Kinect¬†[71]) to capture RGB and depth images (see Figure¬†3). The touchpad (Sensel Morph), measuring 240‚Äâ√ó\times√ó‚Äâ169.5‚Äâmm, is mounted on a tripod head. We use four different texture overlays (white, green, dark wood, light wood) printed on paper and placed over the Sensel Morph pad across participants. The seven static Azure Kinect cameras are attached to the aluminum frame, and the head-mounted camera is fixed on a helmet. The frame also holds a computer display and is surrounded by a green screen.

All cameras and the touchpad are connected to two workstations (Intel Core i7-9700K, Nvidia GeForce RTX 3070), their timestamps are synchronized via a Raspberry Pi CM4 using PTP, which also triggers all Azure Kinect cameras simultaneously at a frame rate of 30‚Äâfps.

Head-mounted camera tracking

To obtain accurate poses of the head-mounted camera, we attach nine active infrared markers around the Sensel Morph pad in an asymmetric layout (see Figure¬†3). These markers, controlled by the Raspberry Pi CM4, can be extracted from the Azure Kinect‚Äôs infrared image using simple thresholding (saturating the range of values of the infrared camera). The markers are turned on simultaneously, allowing for the computation of the camera pose via Perspective-N-Points and enabling an accurate evaluation of the temporal synchronization between cameras and the touchpad.

We recruited 21 participants from our institution (6 female, 15 male, ages 23‚Äì32 years, mean age‚Äâ=‚Äâ26 years), ensuring a broad representation to cover anatomic differences in hand characteristics. 3.3 Data acquisition procedure

Participants sat on an adjustable stool in front of the apparatus, wearing a helmet with a mounted camera pointing towards the Sensel Morph and a black hand stocking on each arm up to the wrist. Before starting the data capture, the experimenter explained the task and the purpose of the study. They then signed a consent form (see Appendix) and provided demographic information. The participants first performed a calibration gesture by slowly turning each hand, with fingers spread, within the camera rig. After calibration, participants conducted 31 different gestures, including touch, press, and drag gestures of varying strength, with each hand on the Sensel Morph touchpad (see Appendix for a description of gestures). Each gesture was repeated 5 times if it involved a single touch action (e.g., press index finger) and 3 times if it involved a sequence of sequential touches (e.g., draw letters). Before each gesture, participants watched a video demonstrating how to perform the corresponding gesture with written instructions on a computer monitor in front of them. The experimenter guided the participants throughout the study, which took around 1 hour per participant. Participants could take a voluntary break after each gesture and received a chocolate bar as gratitude for their participation. In total, we recorded 6216 different gestures, i.e., 21‚Äâparticipants‚Äâ√ó\times√ó‚Äâ2‚Äâhands‚Äâ√ó\times√ó‚Äâ(1‚Äâcalibration + 27‚Äâ√ó\times√ó‚Äâ5 + 4‚Äâ√ó\times√ó‚Äâ3) gestures.

The average length of each motion sequence is 14 seconds, with an almost equal balance between frames capturing the left and right hands. Figure¬†4 shows the mean sequence lengths across gestures. 5 Benchmark Evaluation

While previous work demonstrated the advantages of using RGB images to estimate pressure maps, the method of estimating them with RGBs and hand poses, which are essential to understanding hand manipulation, remains unexplored.

In this section, we introduce a new benchmark for estimating hand pressure using both RGB images and 3D hand poses, which will be valuable for future applications and research in AR/VR and robotics. we further establish baselines to illustrate that combining hand poses and RGB images enhances the estimation of hand pressure.

In the subsequent sections, we detail the architectures of our baselines for RGB only and RGB + hand pose models, the evaluation metrics, implementation details and the results from these baselines using our dataset.

5.1 Network Architecture

Our model builds upon PressureVisionNet¬†[1], We enhance the encoder-decoder segmentation architectures, which conventionally employ RGB image format by adding an additional dimension to the channel. Specifically, we project 3D hand joints onto the image plane with z values from the egocentric camera at a millimeter scale. Following the approach of PressureVision¬†[1], we frame the pressure estimation as a classification problem, dividing the pressure range into nine discrete bins.

As our setup uses a dynamic camera from an egocentric view, we preprocess the images by cropping them with a margin around the hand, applying random positional augmentation, and resizing them to fit the size of network inputs. . For the validation, we locate the hand in the center of the frame during the cropping process without random positional augmentation.

5.2 Implementation Details

For the evaluation metrics, we report contact IoU, volumetric IoU, mean absolute error to measure spatial pressure quality, and temporal accuracy to evaluate contact temporal consistency, same as in PressureVision¬†[1]. We select our dataset split by participants, with three allocated to the test set, and seven to the training set. The baselines are optimized using Adam¬†[80] with an initial learning rate of 1e-3 for the first 100k iterations and 1e-4 for the remaining iterations. Training took approximately 6.5 hours using an Nvidia GeForce GTX 4090. The batch size is set to 8, and the input dimensions for the network are 480‚Äâx‚Äâ384‚Äâx‚Äâ4 for RGB-only and 480‚Äâx‚Äâ384‚Äâx‚Äâ4 for RGB and hand pose. While the original input dimensions for images are 1920 x 1080, we crop and resize the image around hands with a margin of 150 pixels around the hand-bounding box. During the training, we apply random augmentations to the hand-bounding boxes up to 50 pixels in each direction. However, we do not perform any augmentations during the evaluation phase. Our baselines are trained solely on images from the egocentric camera.

5.3 Results

We provide baseline results with different modalities for our dataset in Table¬†2. We employ the hand pose modality as a form of projected hand joints on the images with z-axis information from the egocentric camera coordinate. The results indicate that employing the hand pose modality improves 5.51% in contact IoU, 7.12% in volumetric IoU on Egocentric views. In Figure¬†7, we illustrate the qualitative results of pressure estimation by the baselines.

While both methods successfully estimate pressure in simple scenarios, such as single-finger interactions, the RGB-only method finds it more challenging with multi-finger interactions involving self-hand occlusion of contacting joints. Overall, we have observed that our model shows a notable improvement in performance, particularly for gestures involving multiple fingers and higher force values. This is in contrast to gestures that use single digits and lower force. Specifically, Table¬†3 presents the performance across five different gestures groups. We see a greater improvement in volumetric IoU for multi-finger interactions (7.6%), pinch finger interaction (6.8%) and palm interaction (6.3%) compared to single finger interaction (3.3%) and grasp interaction (1.3%). We hypothesize that the improved performance for certain gestures may be due to increased self-occlusion of the interacting fingertips, where the additional hand pose input plays a critical role in enhancing robustness

We observed a similar improvement when evaluating our model on data from static cameras, specifically using images from cameras 2, 3, 4, and 5 for training and testing (see Table¬†2). When testing the same model (trained on images from cameras 2, 3, 4, and 5) on unseen views from cameras 1, 6, and 7 (i.e., images not included in the training set), our model also showed significant improvement over PressureVision¬†[1]‚Äîalthough with a drop in performance compared to the results on known viewpoints. This suggests that incorporating pose information enhances pressure prediction across different camera perspectives.

We conducted additional benchmarks using the HaMeR hand pose estimates. While they do improve pressure map estimation, our optimized hand poses consistently outperform them across all metrics: 4.3% in Contact IoU, 2.6% in MAE, 2.1% in Vol IoU, and 0.54% in Temporal Accuracy (Table¬†2 ). This highlights the crucial role of precise hand poses in predicting pressure maps.

6 Societal Impact

The results of this project are intended to improve the interaction between human hands and digital interfaces, particularly in AR/VR environments. As such, we foresee these results bringing significant benefits in terms of enhancing user experience and facilitating more natural and intuitive interactions in digital spaces. We do not anticipate any social or ecological risks arising from our study. We strictly adhered to ethical guidelines throughout the study, ensuring participant safety, comfort, and confidentiality.

7 Conclusion

In this paper, we introduce EgoPressure, a novel egocentric hand pressure dataset paired with a multi-view hand pose estimation method. EgoPressure includes precise hand poses with meshes, multi-view RGB and depth images, egocentric view images, and high-quality pressure data. We establish a new benchmark and demonstrate the effectiveness of using hand pose data in pressure estimation. For future work, we plan to enhance our dataset by including objects to enable pressure estimation on more complex geometries. In conclusion, we believe that EgoPressure represents a significant step towards a deeper machine understanding of hand-object interactions from egocentric views.

Appendix C Extended Details about Benchmark Evaluation

C.1 Network Backbone

To implement our baseline for the benchmark, we utilize an encoder-decoder segmentation architecture. Specifically, we employ an ImageNet-pretrained Squeeze-and-Excitation Network (SEResNeXt50)¬†[89, 90] as the encoder, which takes both RGB and 3D hand pose inputs, and a feature pyramid network¬†[91, 92] as the decoder, which generates a pressure map.

C.2 Cropping Method

In preprocessing, images are cropped with a margin around the hand, random augmentations are applied, and the images are resized to fit the network input dimensions. During validation, we ensure the hand remains centrally positioned in the frame throughout the cropping process.

C.3 Evaluation Metrics

For evaluation, we follow the four metrics used in PressureVision¬†[1]: Contact Intersection over Union (IoU), Volumetric IoU, Mean Absolute Error (MAE), and Temporal Accuracy.

Contact IoU evaluates the accuracy of contact surface predictions by calculating the IoU between the estimated and ground truth pressure binarized contact maps. Volumetric IoU assesses the accuracy of pressure magnitude by comparing the sum of lower values to the sum of higher values between the estimated and ground truth pressures. MAE measures the accuracy of pressure in physical units per pixel. Temporal Accuracy evaluates the consistency of contact over time by verifying frame-by-frame contact consistency between the estimated and ground truth values.

Appendix D Limitations

Although EgoPressure serves as a foundational study for understanding pressure from an egocentric view, several challenges remain unresolved. These challenges are categorized into three main areas.

First, measuring pressure while interacting with objects presents a challenge. The current pressure sensor is limited to flat surfaces. Future research could explore the measurement of pressure on objects that are movable, compliant, and have more complex geometries.

Second, the current dataset was only captured in an indoor setting. Expanding the dataset to include outdoor settings and more diverse backgrounds would enhance its usability for real-world applications.

Finally, the current setup only considers single-hand interactions. Incorporating scenarios involving the use of both hands would be a natural extension of our work.

Addressing these challenges in future research would improve pressure estimation in real-world scenarios and broaden its applicability.
