Abstract

We present a generic algorithm for scoring pose estimation methods that rely on single image semantic analysis. The algorithm employs a lightweight putative shape representation using a combination of multiple Gaussian Processes. Each Gaussian Process (GP) yields distance normal distributions from multiple reference points in the object’s coordinate system to its surface, thus providing a geometric evaluation framework for scoring predicted poses. Our confidence measure comprises the average mixture probability of pixel back-projections onto the shape template. In the reported experiments, we compare the accuracy of our GP based representation of objects versus the actual geometric models and demonstrate the ability of our method to capture the influence of outliers as opposed to the corresponding intrinsic measures that ship with the segmentation and pose estimation methods.

1 Introduction

The recovery of object pose from single images is a problem that arises in a variety of applications in robotics and computer vision and constitutes a topic of ongoing scrutiny. In the case of rigid objects, it allows the estimation of the object’s 3D location and 3D orientation in the coordinate system of the camera, also referred to as the 6D object pose. With the recent developments in machine learning, neural network based solutions have demonstrated significant efficiency, particularly in terms of detecting the 6D pose of objects from a single image with respect to some latent representation of the corresponding templates, e.g. [nguyen2023nope, hodan2020epos, rambach2018learning]. Several works provide comprehensive reviews of such methods (e.g. [2021Asurvey, Zhu2022]), usually categorizing 6D object pose estimation according to the prior information, the number of views and the type of methods exploited. Drawing from the outcomes of the BOP Challenges [sundermeyer], it is notable that while deep learning methods exhibited inferior performance compared to classical methods in 2018, nowadays they dominate the field. Significant accuracy improvement was demonstrated between 2020 and 2023, with the average recall of the best performing method to increase from 0.69 in 2020 to 0.85 in 2023. Methods have evolved to exploit RGB and RGBD data, both appearance and geometric information, tackling the problem of multiple object instances in the scene, objects with weak textures and symmetries [hodan2020epos] and by generalizing to estimate the pose of unseen objects, addressing category-level 6D pose estimation in a fully [lin2021] or a semi-supervised manner [Lin2022]. The evaluation of 6D pose estimation methods typically resides on an established offline procedure exploiting ground truth benchmark data and state of the art metrics. Though certain cases necessitate quantifying the uncertainty of the estimated 6D pose at runtime. The most prominent examples stem from robot grasping and manipulation tasks, in which perception-action loops are exploited. A prominent challenge in robotic planning tasks aiming at grasping or following a real-world object is to have a means of evaluating the degree of confidence in the measured 6D pose of the target. In this paper, we tackle this problem over object pose estimates from single images.To do so, we leverage geometric consistency between the detected object and its 3D shape template via the estimated pose in order to compute a confidence value. To construct the template, we devised a lightweight Gaussian process mixture model, where each GP is trained on a specific region of the target surface. The idea of classifier mixtures has been previously introduced in [has2023gradient] for simple regression problems, yet, to the best of our knowledge, this is the first time a GP mixture model is used for shape representation.

2 Contributions

We introduce a consistent, method-independent confidence measure for pose estimates relying on shape discrepancy in the local object coordinate frame. The contribution of our method is two-fold.

  1. 1.

    It provides a consistent and unbiased measure of quality for the pose estimate by assessing the consistency of the back-projected image of the object against an accurate shape template.

  2. 2.

    It introduces a novel geometric method involving a mixture of GP based priors to obtain “lightweight” explicit shape representations from sparse samples of the target (object). The accuracy of the representation is sufficient to yield reliable confidence values.

3 Related work

Related work focuses on the following areas: shape representation, 6D object pose evaluation and uncertainty estimation

3.1 Shape representation

The representation of 3D shapes is a fundamental problem for various applications in 3D computer vision, prompting the exploration of different methodologies to encode object geometry. Point clouds, meshes, and voxels are the most popular explicit representations able to capture arbitrary amounts of detail in the shapes of objects. However, these representations tend to grow in size (i.e, larger numbers of points or vertices/faces are required) with the level of detail. Implicit representations are parametric functional models that are more storage-efficient than the traditional ones and have proven to be adept at learning complex topologies. With recent advances in deep neural networks, learned implicit representations have shown promising results. Farshian et al. [Farshian2023] provide an extensive overview of related methods to date. These methods represent shape as either a learned feature vector, coupled with a trained decoder related to explicit representations such as point clouds [achlioptas2017learning, Qi2017], meshes [Wang2018_pixel2mesh], voxels  [Brock2016], or represent shape with an implicit surface function. Deep implicit functions utilize an input observation as a latent vector and train a neural network to estimate binary occupancy [Mescheder2019] or a signed-distance function [park2019deepsdf] at a given query location in 3D space. NERFs [Mindenhall_NeRF] can be regarded as such an approach with respect to occupancy. Similarly, kernel-based methods like Neural Kernel Fields (NKF) [williams2021nkf] and Neural Kernel Surface Reconstruction (NKSR) [huang2023nksr] predict occupancy values but leverage learned kernel functions, which provide a strong inductive bias for reconstructing shapes from sparse data. Deep implicit functions have gained popularity and significantly advanced the state of the art in 3D shape generation [Mescheder2019, park2019deepsdf, chen_implicit_fields2019, Genova2019LocalDI], 3D shape completion [Chibane2020ImplicitFI], 4D reconstruction [Niemeyer2019OccupancyF4], and 3D shape correspondence [LiuIF2024]. Several extensions have been proposed, including learning separate implicit functions for shape parts [Paschalidou2020LearningUH] or integrating a deformation model for shape registration [zheng2021deep]. While these methods generally provide compact shape representations with low dimensional latent vectors, they however suffer from the typical short-comings of regression models, such as under-fitting and inability to cope with missing data points. Furthermore, the use of implicit fields requires an underlying geometric setup that will enable transition the from points to field values and vice versa. On the other hand explicit representations complicate their deployment in deep learning due to their irregular (meshes) or even unordered (point clouds) nature of data types and existing works are either restricted to small deviations from a 3D template or graph or small number of vertices/ points or simple convex shapes restricted to limited output quality [Farshian2023, Liao2018]. The work of Dragiev et al. [dragiev2011], is, to the best of our knowledge, the only use-case of Gaussian processes for implicit shape regression with application to grasping. The GPs are trained over a signed distance to the object’s surface in a given local reference frame.

3.2 6D pose evaluation and uncertainty estimation

The most prominent approaches adopted in literature for evaluating 6D pose estimation methods relies on available benchmark ground truth data and state of the art metrics such as VSD, MSSD, MSPD, ADD [hoda] or 3-D IoU [wang2019]. When 6D pose estimation is exploited in actionable tasks, i.e. perception-to-action robotic tasks, such as grasping, the rates of successful grasps are commonly computed as post-action performance indicators. However, in order to proactively avoid executing a potentially unnecessary grasping plan that was based on a weak pose estimate, a confidence measure over the object pose estimate is required. Works pertaining to the evaluation of the quality of pose estimates fall under the broader research area of uncertainty quantification in robotics, or more particularly, predictive uncertainty, wherein a measure of “action risk” is computed reflecting uncertainty in the underlying pose estimate. The majority of works along this research strand aim to endow deep pose estimation models with the capability to directly assess and quantify the uncertainty of their own predictions by directly training the model to output a confidence score [tremblay18, loquercio2020]. In principle, these are data-dependent approaches based on learning, and subsequently suffer from inherent bias related to plurality and variation in the training data. In other words they exhibit limited robustness [blum_fishyscapes_2021] and occasional tendency to overfit, thereby yielding overconfident estimates [Shi2021] and a deviation from the ground-truth uncertainty is often observable. Furthermore, they tend to be computationally expensive. The method of [Shi2021] exploits ensemble methods [Lakshminarayanan2017], which in general tend to be more lightweight in terms of inference time. In [Shi2021], the problem is approached by an ensemble of different models optimizing translation/rotation/ADD errors and computing the average residuals as the uncertainty measure.

4 Method

The core idea of our method is to use putative 3D representations of objects (henceforth referred to as “templates”) that we are anticipating our classifier to detect and subsequently predict their pose. These classifiers typically ship with measures of self-assessed confidence that provide some indication of quality for the estimated pose. However, these quality measures bear the inherent bias of the self-assessment process and can therefore be misleading for the back-end application. To eliminate this bias, we resort to the use of lightweight object templates to validate the geometric consistency of the estimated object pose. Put differently, we utilize the pose estimate to generate a partial 3D reconstruction of the detected object and establish a confidence score by assessing the deviations of the reconstructed points from the template surface.

4.1 Shape representation with Gaussian processes

As discussed earlier in sec. 3.1 3D objects can be represented with pointclouds or meshes. With the former, large numbers of points are required, usually in the order of 100,000 or more, whereas the latter are significantly more lightweight, but with occasional loss of detail in surface regions represented by large polygons. The shortcomings of these representations with respect to the task at hand, i.e., the comparison between the reconstructed object against a template shape pertain to storage and time complexity that such a comparison entails. In particular, where point clouds are concerned, to evaluate the distance of a given point from the surface would require nearest neighbor queries for a large number of points, which, in turn, requires a large storage (e.g., a KD-tree) of tightly spaced points. Similarly, for mesh representations, such a query would require the determination of the polygon nearest to the given point, thereby incurring substantial spatial search. A comparison between the GP-based and KD-Tree representations is shown in Table 1 where the GP-based representation was derived by training with a sparse set of 500 points and the KD-tree was constructed with the same set of points. Both methods were evaluated using 3200 unseen points. The trained GP models can generalize and continuously predict the distance for every given direction. In contrast, KD-Tree can only query the nearest sparse neighbors and thus lead to a greater MSE error. Although the storage requirement of the GP based representation is higher than that of the KD-tree, it remains relatively low, and the estimated inference time is still efficient.

4.1.3 Surface coverage with mixtures of distance likelihoods

It becomes evident that for objects with more complex shapes, a single GP model will not suffice, because there would exist rays emanating from the reference point that intercept the exterior surface of the object in multiple points (e.g., Fig. 3). 5 Data preparation and training

To train our model, we first normalize the input mesh to a unit sphere similarly to  [park2019deepsdf] and densely sample points on its surface. This is done by rendering the object from different viewpoints (e.g. forming a Fibonacci sphere around the object) and ray-casting to its surface. From these points, we typically use a sparse subset (e.g. 10,000) for training and an unseen set of points for testing (e.g. 30000). To train the GP models, we define the number of reference points, partition the point cloud using distance-based clustering (see 4.1.3) and parameterize the 3D points as directions and distances 2). 6 Results

Our experimentation has dual scope: a) To assess the effectiveness of the confidence score in identifying a good pose and, b) to demonstrate the efficiency of the template. For the latter, we use various models from the ShapeNet dataset as well as objects from an industrial workspace wherein accurate robotic grasping of objects is a key operational feature.

6.1 Template accuracy, fidelity and comparative assessment

Table 2 shows the quantitative comparison and evaluation of the shape representation task of our method against DeepSDF [park2019deepsdf] and NKSR [huang2023nksr] for three different categories of the ShapeNet dataset, namely planes, chairs and sofas. We used 120 models from each of the above categories to evaluate the shape representation results of the three methods using the following metrics: the Chamfer Distance (CD) for point cloud similarity, the Precision (P𝑃Pitalic_P) for quantifying the accuracy of the reconstruction (i.e. how close the reconstructed points lie to the ground truth), the Recall (R𝑅Ritalic_R) for quantifying the reconstruction’s completeness (i.e. percentage of the ground truth points that are covered by the reconstruction and the F𝐹Fitalic_F (F-score). As shown in Table 2, our method achieves the lowest CD and over 90% F-score in all cases. Similarly, high Precision and Recall are attained in all cases, while in the the case of the planes category our model achieves 93.6% Recall. In the qualitative comparison of Fig. 5 it is clearly visible that DeepSDF and NKSR over-smooth or fail to reconstruct regions with discontinuities and shape details, while our method is able to capture the overall shape with its details more robustly.

Note that these are intrinsically different methods. While DeepSDF and NKSR excel in generalizing across different objects, the former by learning the latent space of shapes and the latter by combining the conditioning of kernel parameters on the data with kernel ridge regression, our method is more adept in representing details of a specific object. Clearly, the number and locations of the reference points may impact the fidelity of the template. Specifically on the plurality of reference points, an ablation study shown in Fig. 7 indicates that beyond a cutoff, the representation error (i.e. Chamfer distance) begins to drop. Note however that that their location may have a significant impact in the quality of the representation. Overall, perturbing the number of reference points and their locations could lead to improvements, particularly when dealing with complex objects. For instance, in the case of the chair depicted in Fig. 6, comparing results obtained from clustering-based estimation of reference points (cf. sect.  4.1) with those manually defined, a more accurate shape representation is derived. In the same figure, the results of DeepSDF and NKSR for the same object are contrasted affirming the loss of object details.

7 Conclusion & Future Work

This paper puts forward a novel score for the quality of pose estimates of objects captured in images, based on geometric consistency with template shape. We also propose a method to build this template using Gaussian processes. Our results demonstrate that the representation is sufficiently accurate to facilitate the computation of a reliable confidence score that reflects the true quality of the estimated pose. In future work, we aim at endowing the shape representation algorithm with theoretical guarantees for full coverage of the object surface irrespective of shape complexity via learned mixtures models and fully automating the choice of reference points.

10 Hardware and softaware details

Our pipeline is fully GPU-accelerated, leveraging GPytorch and CUDA. All experiments were conducted using a single RTX 4090 GPU with 24GB of VRAM. Introducing overlapping regions increases the overall number of training samples.
