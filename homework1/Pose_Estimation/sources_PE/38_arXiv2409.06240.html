Test-Time Certifiable Self-Supervision to Bridge the Sim2Real Gap in
Event-Based Satellite Pose Estimation

Mohsi Jawaid†, Rajat Talak∗, Yasir Latif†, Luca Carlone∗ and Tat-Jun Chin† †Sentient Satellites Laboratory (SSL), Australian Institute for Machine Learning (AIML), The University of Adelaide, SA 5005, Australia.∗Laboratory for Information and Decision Systems (LIDS), Massachusetts Institute of Technology, Cambridge, MA 02139, USA.
Abstract

Deep learning plays a critical role in vision-based satellite pose estimation. However, the scarcity of real data from the space environment means that deep models need to be trained using synthetic data, which raises the Sim2Real domain gap problem. A major cause of the Sim2Real gap are novel lighting conditions encountered during test time. Event sensors have been shown to provide some robustness against lighting variations in vision-based pose estimation. However, challenging lighting conditions due to strong directional light can still cause undesirable effects in the output of commercial off-the-shelf event sensors, such as noisy/spurious events and inhomogeneous event densities on the object. Such effects are non-trivial to simulate in software, thus leading to Sim2Real gap in the event domain. To close the Sim2Real gap in event-based satellite pose estimation, the paper proposes a test-time self-supervision scheme with a certifier module. Self-supervision is enabled by an optimisation routine that aligns a dense point cloud of the predicted satellite pose with the event data to attempt to rectify the inaccurately estimated pose. The certifier attempts to verify the corrected pose, and only certified test-time inputs are backpropagated via implicit differentiation to refine the predicted landmarks, thus improving the pose estimates and closing the Sim2Real gap. Results show that the our method outperforms established test-time adaptation schemes.

I Introduction

On-orbit servicing (OOS) includes a range of space-based activities such as refuelling, maintenance, assembly and debris removal [1]. By promoting reusability, extendability and space environmental protection, OOS is crucial for sustainable space utilisation. However, the viability of OOS depends on conducting space operations safely and cost effectively, which argues for a high degree of autonomy.

Underpinning OOS are autonomous spacecraft rendezvous and docking capabilities, which require accurate and real-time estimation of the 6DoF pose of a target satellite relative to the servicer satellite in proximity range. To this end, vision-based satellite pose estimation has established its great potential [2], where the monocular case is a leading option due to its lower size, weight and power demands.

State-of-the-art vision-based satellite pose estimators employ deep learning [3, 4, 5, 6], either in an end-to-end manner or in combination with other computer vision techniques [7]. However, the scarcity of real data from the actual operating environment means that the deep models are often trained using synthetic data [8, 9]. This generates a simulation-to-real (Sim2Real) gap [10] between the training and testing domains, which raises doubts on whether the trained pose estimators can operate successfully in space.

A major cause of Sim2Real gap in vision-based satellite pose estimation are previously unseen before lighting conditions in space. It is well known that the major sources of illumination in orbit, i.e., the Sun and the Earth’s albedo [11], can produce undesirable effects on the images of satellites in orbit, e.g., high contrast, camera over-exposure, stray light and lens flare [10]. Such effects are also non-trivial to simulate in software, which further contributes to the Sim2Real gap. To bridge the gap, domain adaptation methods [12] have been applied in satellite pose estimation [9, 5, 6].

Another approach to mitigate the effects of novel lighting is by using optical sensors that are less susceptible to illumination variations, specifically, the event sensor [13]. Since event sensors asynchronously detect intensity changes, they have much higher dynamic ranges. Previous works showed that event sensors exhibited a high degree of sensing robustness across drastic lighting changes [14, 15]. The potential benefits of event sensing to space applications have been recognised [16, 17, 18]. In particular, Jawaid et al. [19] established the viability of training a 6DoF satellite pose estimator that operates on event frames, with initial results suggesting that the Sim2Real gap for event data is smaller; see Fig. 1 (cols. 2 and 3). We will provide empirical evidence to further support the claim in Sec. VI.

However, while event sensors offer a degree of resilience against lighting variability, harsh illumination conditions can still cause undesirable effects on commercial off-the-shelf (COTS) event sensors. In particular, non-uniform lighting due to strong directional light sources can lead to varying contrasts, which manifest as non-uniform event densities on an object; see Fig. 1 (col. 4). The pattern of real events generated are difficult to synthetically generate, which results in a domain gap for models trained on synthetic event data that did not include the effects of harsh lighting; see Fig. 2.

I-A Contributions

To deal with the Sim2Real gap in the event domain due to harsh lighting on COTS event sensors, we propose a certifiable self-supervised scheme [20, 21] to adapt the event-based pose estimator at test time. The main idea is to employ a certifier that leverages prior knowledge of the target satellite to aim to verify the correctness of estimated poses in a self-supervised manner. Only certified testing inputs are backpropagated via implicit differentiation to bridge the Sim2Real gap at test time. Departing from [20, 21] who focussed on 3D point clouds, we developed a novel certifier that is optimised for the characteristics of 2D event frames (details in Sec. IV-B). See Fig. 2 for a preview.

Compared to previous unsupervised domain adaptation methods for satellite pose estimation [5, 6], our method is source-free (whereas [5] requires the original labelled training data in the adaptation) and refines all learnable weights (whereas [6] updates only the batch norm layers in the CNN [22]). Moreover, [5, 6] need to solve auxiliary tasks (e.g., semantic segmentation) to provide additional guidance, whereas our pipeline conducts only pose estimation.

To evaluate our ideas, we developed a novel dataset for event-based satellite pose estimation. Our new dataset is an improvement over the SEENIC dataset in [19], in that real event data from emulated satellite rendezvous trajectories were captured alongside real RGB data, with both channels accompanied with common calibrated ground truth poses.

II Related work

II-A Satellite pose estimation

In uncooperative rendezvous and docking, the servicer spacecraft must estimate the 6DoF pose of the target spacecraft (e.g., a defunct satellite) without receiving communication or assistance from the target [23]. Sensing modalities that have been explored for the task include radar, LiDAR and optical [24]. Monocular vision is attractive due to its lower size, weight and power demands, especially on nanosatellites which are limited in those respects [2].

The importance of monocular satellite pose estimation as a research topic is underlined by several datasets [25, 10, 8] and challenges [26, 27, 9]. Early adopters of deep learning for the task include Sharma et al. [28, 3]. Pauly et al. [7] grouped existing deep learning approaches into two broad categories: hybrid modular approaches and direct end-to-end approaches. The former category of methods (e.g., [4, 29, 5]) employ classical vision techniques (e.g., perspective-n-point solver) alongside deep learning capabilities (e.g., object detection, landmark regression), while the latter category (e.g., [28, 3, 6]) directly regresses the pose using a CNN without intermediate non-learning stages.

II-B Self-supervised object pose estimation

Self-supervision has been employed to bridge the domain gap between training and testing. Wang et al. [30, 31] trained an RGB-D-based pose estimator on synthetic data and refined it with self-supervised training on real unlabelled data, with the self-supervision signal created through differentiable rendering. An iterative student-teacher scheme was proposed by Chen et al. [32] to bridge the Sim2Real gap. Wang et al. [33] extracted pose-invariant features for category-level object pose estimation of unseen object instances. Differentiable rendering of signed distance fields was utilised by Zakharov et al. [34], together with normalised object coordinate spaces [33], to learn 9D cuboids in a self-supervised manner. Zhang et al. [35] proposed to jointly reconstruct the 3D shape of an object category and learn dense 2D-3D correspondences between the input image and the 3D shape, leveraging large-scale real-world object videos for training. Deng et al. [36] employed a manipulator to interact with the objects in the scene, which generates new data to fine-tune the object pose estimator.

Departing from previous approaches that institute multiple prediction heads that solve complementary tasks, Talak et al. [20, 21] advanced the certifiable self-supervision idea, where a geometric optimisation problem is solved to correct for prediction errors, while a certifier tries to verify the correction and only allows good testing samples to be backprogated via implicit differentiation to adapt the network.

As mentioned in Sec. I-A, domain adaptation is crucial for satellite pose estimation [5, 6]. The advantages of our approach over [5, 6] have been outlined in Sec. I-A.

II-C Event sensing for space applications

While event sensing has received a significant amount of attention in robotics and AI, the potential of event sensing for space applications is only starting to be recognised [16]. Many of the applications relate to guidance, navigation and control (GNC) systems of spacecraft and space robots, such as star tracking and attitude estimation [37, 38], time-to-contact estimation for spacecraft landing [39, 18], and visual odometry for planetary robots [17]. Azzalini et al. [40] investigated the generation of synthetic events for optimal landing trajectories. To gain a better idea of the prospects of event sensing for space applications, see [16].

II-D Event datasets for satellite pose estimation

As alluded to above, Jawaid et al. [19] first considered event sensing for satelite pose estimation, and a dataset was also contributed by them [41]. We address some shortcomings of this data, in particular we now provide equivalent RGB data as well as calibrated 6DoF object poses instead of just camera poses. Another dataset was recently proposed by Rathinam et al. [42], however at the time of submission, their dataset has not been released publicly, hence the precise characteristics were unknown, not to mention the inability to evaluate our method on their dataset.

V Experimental setup

V-A 3D model of target satellite

We designed a 3D model of a satellite-like object (see Fig. 1) that is geometrically similar to satellites employed in previous satellite pose estimation works [10, 25].

In order to focus on the effects of lighting variations on the Sim2Real gap, the following design choices were made:

  • •

    The CAD model was textureless to preclude as much as possible the contributions of discrepancies in the digital and physical textures to the Sim2Real gap.

  • •

    The CAD model was specified to be of a homogeneous material which was hand tuned in Blender [46] to closely match the reflectance of 3D printed PLA surfaces.

V-B Synthetic RGB and event datasets for training

The model in Sec. V-A was rendered using Blender. The model was moved smoothly towards the virtual camera while rotating the camera about the y and z axes simultaneously. Then, the camera was kept static and the model was rotated individually about the x, y and z axes and simultaneously about all pairs of the x, y and z axes. This yielded 2,52025202,5202 , 520 sequential rendered RGB frames with ground-truth object pose for each frame. Successive poses differ by 1∘superscript11^{\circ}1 start_POSTSUPERSCRIPT ∘ end_POSTSUPERSCRIPT on at most 2 axes. A lighting condition was created with 2 identical lights of 2222 m radius positioned at distances of 2222 m and −22-2- 2 m in the world z axis, and 1111 m in the world y axis. This created even diffused lighting around the object which casted minimal shadows; see Fig. 1 (top left). To generate the synthetic event frames, we employed V2E [47] with the sequential RGB frames as input, and set a matching framerate and exposure duration (e.g., 10 fps and 0.1 s respectively). This ensured that we obtain a one-to-one pairing of the synthetic RGB frames and event frames; see Fig. 1 (col 1).

V-C Real RGB and event datasets for testing

V-C1 Model fabrication

We 3D printed the model in Sec. V-A to the physical dims. of 26262626 cm ×\times× 25252525 cm ×\times× 10 cm.

V-C2 Sensors

We employed a Prophesee EVK1 event camera with a 1280×72012807201280\times 7201280 × 720 pixel event sensor and a Basler Ace 2 conventional camera with a 1920×1200192012001920\times 12001920 × 1200 pixel Sony IMX392 optical sensor. The same Arducam 4–12 mm Varifocal C-Mount lens was used for both cameras, with the focal length tuned separately to ensure similar pixel coverage of the target object given the object physical distance in our laboratory.

V-C3 Lighting conditions

The printed 3D model was imaged under 3 lighting conditions:

  • •

    neutral: illuminated with evenly-lit ambient lighting.

  • •

    low: illuminated with 1 weak light source.

  • •

    harsh: illuminated with 1 ring light close to the object.

See Fig. 1 (cols 2–4). low provided a dimly lit environment. harsh was challenging due to strong directional light, high sensor noise, occlusions due to shadows, and lens flares when the light source was within the field-of-view of the camera.

V-C4 Trajectories

Following [19], two trajectories named approach and orbit were executed using a Universal Robots UR5 robot manipulator. approach simulated a docking manoeuvre where the camera linearly approached the satellite, which allowed to evaluate translation errors. The orbit trajectory simulated the chaser flying by the target, which allowed to evaluate rotation errors. See [19, Fig. 5] for an illustration of the trajectories. Both of the trajectories were executed in fast and slow speeds, where fast was 5×5\times5 × higher speed and acceleration than slow. The different speeds allowed to investigate the effects of motion blur.

VII Conclusions

We captured a novel synthetic dataset consisting of RGB and event frames as well as accompanying ground-truth poses extensively covering the expected orientations of the satellite model for training. A new event and RGB dataset was also captured in real-life lab settings with several new conditions to extend our previous work in [19]. These datasets were evaluated on the same base pipeline to conclusively demonstrate the lower Sim2Real domain gap of event sensors as compared to RGB sensors for the task of satellite pose estimation. Moreover, we proposed a test-time certifiable self-supervision scheme to further close the domain gap for event frames. A comparison against established TTA method indicated superior performance of our pipeline.
