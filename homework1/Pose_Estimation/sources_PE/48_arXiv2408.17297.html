BOP-D: Revisiting 6D Pose Estimation Benchmarks for
Better Evaluation under Visual Ambiguities

Abstract

Currently, 6D pose estimation methods are benchmarked on datasets that consider, for their ground truth annotations, visual ambiguities as only related to global object symmetries. However, as previously observed¬†[26], visual ambiguities can also happen depending on the viewpoint or the presence of occluding objects, when disambiguating parts become hidden. The visual ambiguities are therefore actually different across images. We thus first propose an automatic method to re-annotate those datasets with a 6D pose distribution specific to each image, taking into account the visibility of the object surface in the image to correctly determine the visual ambiguities. Given this improved ground truth, we re-evaluate the state-of-the-art methods and show this greatly modify the ranking of these methods. Our annotations also allow us to benchmark recent methods able to estimate a pose distribution on real images for the first time. We will make our annotations for the T-LESS dataset and our code publicly available.

1 Introduction

Visual 6D pose estimation of an object consists in determining the 3D position and orientation of this object with respect to the camera that explain the observed image. It is a key task in many application domains, such as robotics (e.g. grasping or manipulation), augmented reality, industrial quality control, etc. In such contexts, datasets of images with pose ground-truths are of primary importance, since they can be used to train learning based methods, but first and foremost to benchmark the performances of proposed approaches. The annotation accuracy of reference benchmarks, such as BOP¬†[39] for 6D pose estimation, is thus crucial since it influences the research directions.

However, annotating images of objects with their 6D poses is a complex task. This is especially true when considered objects include symmetrical parts. Indeed, as illustrated in Figure¬†1, an image does not necessarily correspond to a single pose solution depending on the viewpoint and occlusions. While symmetrical objects naturally imply multiple solutions, non-symmetrical objects can also yield multiple solutions in case of partial occlusions. Current reference benchmarks¬†[39] ignore these occlusion-induced symmetries, resulting in an imperfect evaluation of the methods. As we will show, considering better ground truth distributions results in a significant change of ranking of current pose estimation methods.

In this paper, we first propose a method that automatically estimates pose distributions for images that take into account the ambiguities due to the object symmetries but also to the partial occlusions. Our method exploits the current annotations as a starting point, the ground truth visibility masks of the objects, and their 3D models. While we demonstrate it on T-LESS, our method is fully automatic and can be applied to other datasets.

We also re-evaluate classical metrics with these annotations to evaluate the performance of pose estimation methods that return a single pose estimate per image. This yields a strongly modified ranking of current challenger methods.

Moreover, we also consider the few methods that already predict multi-modal pose distributions¬†[7, 17]. Because of the absence of proper annotations, these methods have been evaluated quantitatively only on synthetic images and only qualitatively on real images. Thanks to our annotations, we are able to provide their first evaluation on real data.

In summary, our contributions are the following:

  1. 1.

    A novel automatic method computing a multi-modal 6D pose distributions ground truth from a unique ground truth pose and a proposal set of object symmetries;

  2. 2.

    A comprehensive re-evaluation of 6-DOF single pose estimation methods related to the re-annotation of the T-LESS¬†[11] ground-truth;

  3. 3.

    A new evaluation framework of 6-DOF pose distribution estimation methods, which makes it the first multi-modal pose distribution benchmarking on real data.

Our annotations and code will be made public.

2 Related Work

Object 6D pose estimation aims at determining an object pose that best explains the camera observation. While the solution is unique when the object is non-symmetrical and not occluded, multiple equivalent possible solutions arise when the object is symmetrical or when the occlusions prevent the observation of symmetry-breaking elements.

While the state of the art mostly focused on estimating a single pose¬†[4, 35, 13, 21, 38, 41, 6, 37], some recent works focus on estimating the complete set of solutions [26, 33, 30, 20, 27, 16, 7, 17].

As we argue below, to properly evaluate the performance of a method, ground truth annotations including the complete set of solutions for each test image, and metrics that take into account the multiplicity of the solutions, are required, even for methods that returns a single pose per image.

Datasets and 6D pose annotation.

Various techniques have been introduced to annotate image datasets with their 6D poses. For synthetic datasets¬†[2, 40], the ground truth pose is directly available, while, for real datasets, this pose is usually determined with the help of user interaction¬†[5, 12, 3], markers [8, 9, 1], or a robotic arm¬†[42, 28], and refined with ICP in the case of an RGB-D camera¬†[5, 12, 42, 28].

However, such annotations only provide a single pose solution while multiple may exist. For fully symmetric objects, the solutions are always related with the same set of rigid transforms, independently of the viewpoint or the occlusions. This set of transforms, also called symmetries pattern, can then be pre-computed offline and applied to the initial solution to recover the whole set of solutions¬†[14].

For non-symmetrical objects, recovering the complete set of solutions is much more challenging since symmetries may arise from the non-visibility of disambiguating parts of the object, those non-visibilities being induced by the viewpoint (self-occlusion), by other elements of the scene (external occlusion). The transformation set that relates the different solutions is then specific to each image and cannot be pre-computed from the 3D object model.

To our knowledge, no method has been proposed to determine this ground-truth transformation set per image. Instead, the current gold standard in 6D pose estimation benchmarks still consists in approximating this per-image transformation set with a unique global transformation set. The latter is usually computed with the same method than fully symmetric object with large enough tolerance to ignore small disambiguating elements (e.g. surface deviation tolerance of 1.5cm or 10% of the object diameter¬†[14]).

Evaluation metrics.

Performance evaluation usually differs depending on whether the method is used to output a single pose or a distribution.

For single pose estimation, the accuracy is measured through the deviation of the object surface points when transformed by the estimate and by the ground truth pose¬†(registration error)¬†[10]. Depending on the sensor used‚ÄîRGB or RGB-D‚Äîand the targeted application¬†(e.g. robotics or Augmented Reality), deviations can be measured in 3D space (3D distance) or in the image space¬†(reprojection error), and the registration error can be considered as the mean or maximal error over the object surface. In case of multi-valued ground-truths due to a symmetrical object or occlusion-induced symmetries, the accuracy corresponds to the minimal accuracy with respect to the set of solutions¬†[10, 44, 40]. In practice, the most commonly used accuracy measures are :

  1. 1.

    Average Distance metric (ADD), and its variations for symmetrical objects (ADD-S¬†[44], ADD-H¬†[40]), target to measure the mean Euclidean error between the surface points of the object transformed with the estimated pose and the ground truth pose, but have been replaced by MSSD now.

  2. 2.

    Visual Surface Discrepancy (VSD)¬†[12] measures the ratio of pixels for which the discrepancy between their value in the distance map computed with the ground truth pose and their values in the distance map computed with the pose to evaluate. VSD is more expensive to compute than MSSD and MSPD, and requires a depth image. It is now omitted for new tasks as stated by the BOP organizers on their website111https://bop.felk.cvut.cz/method_info/447/.

  3. 3.

    Maximum Symmetry-Aware Surface Distance (MSSD)¬†[14] which is similar to ADD but considers the maximal error instead of the mean error and symmetry management. MSSD provides a 3D error, useful to robotics applications.

  4. 4.

    Maximum Symmetry-Aware Projection Distance¬†(MSPD)¬†[14] measures the maximum reprojection error between the projection of the visible points with the ground-truth pose and their corresponding projection with the pose to evaluate and symmetry management. MSPD provides a reprojection error, useful to augmented reality applications.

Based on these accuracy measures, and inspired from the evaluation of the detection methods, single 6D pose estimation methods are usually evaluated through Precision/Recall. In such evaluation frameworks, an estimated pose is considered as correct if its registration error is below a predefined threshold. The precision corresponds to the rate of correct pose estimations, meaning the ratio of the estimated poses that are correct over the number of estimated poses. The recall corresponds to the rate of correctly registered objects, meaning the ratio between the number of object instances in the dataset whose pose was considered as correct over the number of object instances in the dataset.

Regarding the evaluation of multi-modal 6D pose distribution estimation methods, the accuracy is usually measured as a pose error, meaning the minimal deviation between the inferred rotation and translation and the corresponding nearest ground truth poses¬†[30, 7, 18, 36].

If the method outputs a probability distribution over the whole pose space, the method is usually evaluated through its spread, corresponding to the expectation of the pose error and the log-likelihood between the inferred distribution and the multi-valued solution¬†[30, 7, 18, 36, 16], meaning the sum of the mean log probability at ground truth solutions. While the spread provides a probabilistic measure of the accuracy, the log-likelihood measures how similar the probability distributions are. This penalizes the method if some ground truth solutions are missing in the estimate.

However, not all multi-modal 6D pose estimation methods actually provide a full probability distribution over the whole pose space. Instead, some methods output a set of poses corresponding to some local maxima of the underlying probability distribution¬†[17]. For such methods, performances can be evaluated through the Precision/Recall of the multi-valuated estimation. A pose estimate is then considered as correct with respect to one ground truth pose if its distance in pose space do not exceed a threshold Œ¥ùõø\deltaitalic_Œ¥. The Precision for a given image is then defined as the ratio of the number of estimated poses whose distance have at least one ground truth pose that do not exceed the threshold Œ¥ùõø\deltaitalic_Œ¥ over the total number of estimated poses. The Recall for an object image is then defined as the ratio between the number of ground truth pose whose distance have at least one estimated pose that do not exceed the threshold Œ¥ùõø\deltaitalic_Œ¥ over the total number of ground truth poses. Similarly to the spread and log likelihood, these precision and recall are related to the multi-modal pose distribution.

Our method combines a ground truth pose and a symmetries pattern to represent the pose distribution for each image. Unlike previous works, the symmetries pattern is adjusted to each image, taking into account its specific viewpoint and occluded objects¬†(Section¬†3). Moreover, whereas single pose and multi-modal distribution pose methods are currently evaluated with non-comparable metrics, we introduce new evaluation metrics for multi-modal methods that are homogeneous with single pose methods¬†(Section¬†4).

4 Evaluating 6D Pose Distribution Predictions

Currently, single pose estimation methods and distribution pose estimation methods are evaluated on different datasets¬†(BOP challenge for single poses, SYMSOL for 6D pose distributions) with different metrics (registration error for single pose estimation, pose error for 6D pose distribution).

With our annotation method that provides the full set of 6D pose solutions even on real data, it becomes possible to evaluate both tasks on the same dataset. However, it would be beneficial if both tasks can be evaluated with comparable metrics.

We therefore propose to keep the evaluation process of single pose method unchanged, but on a more accurate ground truth, and to extend it to pose distributions evaluation.

First, whereas error pose is commonly used in 6D pose distribution evaluation process¬†[30, 18, 7], we propose to use the registration error. The latter takes the object 3D shape into account, making the accuracy more meaningful for most of the application. It is also the type of error used by the current gold standard benchmark for single pose¬†[39]. Typically, we suggest to use the Maximal Surface Distance¬†(MSD) and Maximum Projective Distance¬†(MPD) metrics.

Second, we propose to keep the metrics of Precision and Recall, but we adapt them to measure Precision over the whole estimated distribution of poses instead of a unique pose, and Recall over the whole set of solutions instead of considering a pose to be found if one of its multiple solutions was found.

5 Experiments

In this section, after evaluating the performances of our annotation method¬†(Section¬†5.2), we present and discuss the impact of our ground-truth annotations and evaluation metrics onto the evaluation of state-of-the-art solutions for both single pose estimation¬†(Section¬†5.2) and multi-modal pose distribution estimation¬†(Section¬†5.4).

Since our annotation process and metrics are related to objects with intrinsic or occlusion-induced symmetries, we perform our evaluations on the T-LESS dataset. Indeed, among the datasets of the BOP challenge¬†[15], T-LESS appears to be the only dataset to exhibit symmetrical objects, with occlusion-induced symmetries, real data and public ground-truth annotations: ITODD¬†[5] and Homebrewed¬†[19] feature symmetrical objects, with occlusion-induced symmetries, but do not share publicly their ground-truths. HOPE¬†[40], IC-BIN¬†[3] and YCB-V¬†[44] have symmetrical shapes, but texture information disambiguate them completely.

The first experiment focus on a qualitative evaluation of our annotation method¬†(Section¬†5.2). The second on evaluate the impact of our re-annotation of T-LESS Dataset on the performance evaluation of the best performer methods of the BOP benchmark¬†[15] on single pose estimation ¬†(the Multiple instances of Multiple known objects (MiMo) task of BOP)¬†(Section¬†5.3). The last experiment evaluate for the first time methods of 6D pose distribution estimation on a non-synthetic dataset with proper annotations and metrics¬†(Section¬†5.4).

5.1 Implementation details

In our experiments, we sample surface points from the CAD models with a resolutions of 0.5mm. For the pre-computation of elementary symmetries patterns for a given object, we use the per-object symmetries pattern given by the BOP challenge¬†[15] as the initial symmetry candidates, with a tolerance factor œµ‚Å¢-symitalic-œµ-sym\epsilon\text{-sym}italic_œµ -sym set to 1mm.

The object surface visibility ùí±‚Å¢(M)ùí±ùëÄ{\cal V}(M)caligraphic_V ( italic_M ) is computed by Z-buffering using ground truth pose PGTsubscriptùëÉGTP_{\text{GT}}italic_P start_POSTSUBSCRIPT GT end_POSTSUBSCRIPT and the 3D model of the object. For the robust symmetry pattern intersection, the soft intersection tolerance factor œÑùúè\tauitalic_œÑ was experimentally adjusted to 28 3D points.

5.2 Qualitative validation of the method

For the qualitative evaluation of our annotating method, we illustrate the results obtained on some images of the T-LESS dataset in Figure¬†3.

As expected, we can observe the coherence between the distribution and the visibility of the disambiguating elements. Since this dataset does not contain any image with visual ambiguity for the translational part of the pose, the ground truth translation is always reduced to a single 3D point.

5.3 Single Pose Estimation Evaluation

Since May 2023, raw results of BOP Challenge¬†[15] public submissions have become publicly available to allow in-depth analysis. In this experiment, we use them to compare the performance evaluations of state-of-the-art methods on T-LESS for both the original 6D annotations and our annotations. We considered top contenders of the BOP challenge for the pose estimation and pose estimation for unseen objects tasks. We also included two methods for the multi-modal pose distribution estimation task¬†[7, 17] for which we kept only the mode with highest confidence mode. We consider their full predicted distributions in the next subsection. Since these methods are not part of BOP challenge, we retrained them on the T-LESS train set including both photo-realistic synthetic images and real images.

Experimental protocol.

We evaluate the current (July 2024) top contenders of the BOP Challenge on the T-LESS dataset¬†[11] on the pose estimation task and the pose estimation of unseen object task together with [7, 17].

This evaluation is based directly on the raw pose results provided by the contenders and the official evaluation scripts of BOP challenges, both being publicly available on the Challenge website. Following the BOP Challenge, the evaluation is based exclusively on recall. Our method ranking is based on the mean of Recalls computed on MSPD and MSSD accuracies. As discussed in Section¬†2, we do not consider VSD metric as it is now omitted for new tasks in BOP.

Results.

Table¬†1 reports the results of our evaluations. We can observe a large impact of the ground truth on the methods ranking since only the first place in the ranking remains unchanged. For example, regarding the ranking of the pose estimation task, the method Surfemb¬†[6] moves from the 19th place with the original ground truth annotation to the 5th place with our annotation, gaining 14 places. For pose estimation of unseen objects, the difference of ranking can reach 8 places.

We observe large impacts on the recall performances as well. Whereas all the 20 first contenders of pose estimation task have a mean recall up to 80% and even up to 90% for 5 methods with the original annotations, only the 4 methods exceed this score with our annotation, with a majority of method having less than 65%. Similarly, all the 14 top contenders for the pose estimation of unseen objects task have a mean recall that exceed 50% with original annotation whereas only the best one exceed this score once evaluated with our annotations.

It is also interesting to note that the ranking order of variants 1.3, 1.4 and 1.5 of GDRNPP (based on different object detectors, training augmentations, and refinement methods) is changed when evaluated with our ground truth. It underlines that inaccurate ground truth annotation can induce erroneous conclusions in ablation studies.

5.4 Pose Distribution Evaluation

In this experiment, we provide the first evaluation of 6D pose distribution estimation methods on a dataset of real images.

Experimental protocol

Similarly to Section¬†5.3, the methods were trained on the train set of T-LESS that includes both photo-realistic synthetic images and real images.

The ground truth used for this dataset is the same than the one of Section¬†5.3 and is constituted of a discreet set of 6D pose obtained with the 6D pose annotation process introduced in Section¬†3. The performances of the methods are evaluated in Precision and Recall, using accuracy measures MPD and MSD as introduced in Section¬†4.

More details on methods processing and results formatting are given in the supplementary material.

Results

We evaluated SpyroPose¬†[7] and LiePose-Diffusion¬†[17] since they provide 6D pose distribution, unlike [30, 16] that provide distribution only over the rotation.

Quantative results are reported on Table¬†2 whereas qualitative results are provided in the supplementary material.

It appears that LiePose-Diffusion outperforms SpyroPose. Without surprise, performances decrease when MSD accuracy is used since those two methods use RGB images only.

6 Limitations

While our solution improves the pose annotations, the resulting pose distribution depends on some parameters (tolerance factors, sampling of the SE(3) space, etc.). We selected their values carefully. We let the reader check the visualizations in the supplementary material.

Our annotation method relies on a geometric analysis of the scene. It is possible that the sensor resolution, sensor noise, field of view, or motion blur may also affect the visibility of the disambiguating parts. Considering these effects would improve even more the ground truth.

7 Conclusion

For simplicity, most of the 6D pose estimation benchmarks rely on a single 6D pose annotation per image, the latter being completed with a per-object symmetry pattern to transform this unique pose into a distribution. We argued that ignoring the per-image nature of the symmetry pattern is prone to bias in the resulting distribution ground truth and in the resulting performances evaluations.

We then proposed a method to annotate 6D pose distribution with a per-image analysis of the object symmetries. We illustrated that the resulting ground truth is more accurate. Moreover, when using this ground truth to re-evaluate current state-of-the-art methods, we showed that the ranking of these methods changes drastically. We also introduced metrics to evaluate methods that estimate a pose distribution and provided the first evaluation of such methods on real data.

We believe that these results should encourage the 3D vision community to adopt a new outlook on these methods and how well they work in practice, and should guide future research. To facilitate development, we will make our annotations and code publicly available.

2.3 Computation of the Lie-Pose Diffusion¬†[17] Distribution Results

The implementation provided by the authors of Lie-Pose allows to train a network for all objects of T-LESS. The inference phase produces one pose estimate per image crop. We batched the inference phase, with varying seeds. We then merged all these estimates into a single set of poses per image crop. The authors report 1000 runs to produce a distribution. For our evaluation, we ran the code 100 times with different noises to produce Lie-Pose distribution results.

As no pose distribution evaluation existed, the authors of¬†[17] reported only few illustrations of pose distributions results on T-LESS. They evaluated their method as a single pose estimation method, with a single run of the method.

3 Illustrations of Single Pose Re-evaluation

We provide here more cues about the changes in the Single Pose ranking, based on the MSSD and MSPD metrics using our new more accurate ground truth.

To do so, we look at the pose estimates of the method GDRNPP v1.4. When we evaluated GDRNPP v1.4 estimates against our new ground truths, it changed the method ranking from rank 6 to rank 10 in Table¬†1. Even more interestingly, the metrics went down from an MSSD of 88.4 and an MSPD of 90.9 to an MSSD of 60.6 and an MSPD of 63.7.

Figure¬†6 illustrates why MSSD and MSPD changed for this method. It appears that, although GDRNPP v1.4 estimates were close to the ground truth pose, its rotations were not precise. When they are evaluated against a symmetries pattern that is not precise, the evaluation appears correct. Our new ground truth shows that GDRNPP v1.4 tends not to align correctly some of the objects. Hence the drop in performances.
