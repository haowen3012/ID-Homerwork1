Real-Time Estimation of Relative Pose for UAVs Using a Dual-Channel Feature Association

Zhaoying Wang, Wei Dong The authors are with the State Key Laboratory of Mechanical System and Vibration, School of Mechanical Engineering, Shanghai Jiao Tong University, Shanghai 200240, China (e-mail: wangzhaoying@sjtu.edu.cn; dr.dongwei@sjtu.edu.cn;).
Abstract

Leveraging multiple cameras on Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) to form a variable-baseline stereo camera for collaborative perception is highly promising. The critical steps include high-rate cross-camera feature association and frame-rate relative pose estimation of multiple UAVs. However, achieving real-time frame-rate common feature association on the onboard computer with limited computational resources is currently challenging. The time-consuming feature detection and match typically could not be completed within the time of the current frame, occupying time from several subsequent frames. The delay in the feature association not only degrades the real-time performance of relative pose estimation but also causes an asynchronous issue when fusing with local odometry pose, leading to a decrease in the accuracy performance. To accelerate the feature association rate to match the frame rate, we propose a dual-channel structure to decouple the time-consuming feature detection and match from the high-rate image stream. The novel design of periodic guidance and fast prediction effectively utilizes each image frame to achieve a frame-rate feature association. Real-world experiments are executed using SuperPoint and SuperGlue on the NVIDIA NX 8G platform with a 30 Hz image stream. Using single-channel SuperPoint and SuperGlue can only achieve 13 Hz feature association. The proposed dual-channel method can improve the rate of feature association from 13 Hz to 30 Hz, supporting the frame-rate requirement. To accommodate the proposed feature association, we develop a Multi-State Constrained Kalman Filter (MSCKF)-based relative pose estimator in the back-end by fusing the local odometry from two UAVs together with the measurements of common features. Experiments show that the dual-channel feature association improves the rate of visual observation and enhances the real-time performance of back-end estimator compared to the existing methods.

I INTRODUCTION

The collaborative perception of the environment using multiple UAVs has been gaining growing interest[1, 2, 3]. The studies [4, 5, 6] focus on maps fusion and global consistency of trajectories among multiple agents. Other studies [7, 8] explore the benefit of variable baseline of multiple UAV cameras to form a large stereo camera for perception, which is promising to construct the remote environment. There are two critical steps to construct the variable-baseline stereo camera. Firstly, real-time collaborative perception of the environment requires high-rate cross-camera feature association. Secondly, real-time relative pose estimation is crucial for ensuring accurate extrinsic calibration in this multi-camera setup[9]. Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS) can provide the relative pose but is restricted outdoors. Researchers raise interest in using onboard sensors like cameras and IMUs to complete relative pose estimation[10, 11] in general scenarios. [12] and [13] utilize SuperPoint[14] to associate common features and perform Perspective-n-Point (PnP) to calculate the relative pose directly. Due to the time-consuming feature extraction of SuperPoint and the frequent outlier rejection of PnP results, the real-time performance of valid estimation is poor. [15] adopt Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) to improve the real-time performance of state estimation by fusing image-based pose and IMU readings. [16] also constructs the EKF to fuse the local odometry from two UAVs together with the measurements of common features. By gradually minimizing the reprojection error of common features with the predicted pose of local odometry, this approach achieves stable and accurate relative pose estimation in real-time. Whereas the validation is only performed on the desktop computer using one EuRoC[17] sequence for one UAV and the same one for the other UAV with a time offset. When deployed on the UAV platform, the potential challenge is the real-time performance of feature association on the resource-constrained onboard computer[18]. The delay in feature association would dramatically reduce the real-time performance of estimation[19], and cause asynchronous issues with local odometry, leading to a decrease in the accuracy performance.

A suitable feature association algorithm for onboard computing is expected to have real-time capability while ensuring accurate matching performance. [20] thoroughly evaluates the well-known classic and learned feature association algorithms in Scannet[21]. The compared feature descriptors include ORB[22], SIFT[23], SURF[24] and SuperPoint[14]. The compared matching method includes NN matcher and various outlier rejectors: RANSAC[25], PointCN[26], and Order-Aware Network (OANet)[27] as well as learned matcher SuperGlue[20]. Due to the Hpatch adaption in SuperPoint and the attention mechanism in SuperGlue, the combination of SuperPoint with SuperGlue outperforms in precision compared with other approaches. The real-time performance of detectors above are compared in [28]. However, SuperPoint and SuperGlue can not achieve real-time performance on the onboard computer even with acceleration by CUDA and TensorRT[18]. In particular, the time-consuming feature detection and match can not be completed before the arrival of the next image frame, occupying time from subsequent frames. In other words, when the feature association completes, these subsequent image frames after the processed frame are outdated and have to be skipped. The time-consuming feature association is mismatched with the high-rate image stream. Effectively utilizing these skipped image frames to accelerate the rate of feature association will be a promising approach.

In this work, we propose a dual-channel structure in the front-end to accelerate the rate of feature association. The parallel guidance channel and prediction channel decouple the time-consuming feature association from the high-rate image stream. Specifically, the guidance channel conducts the SuperPoint feature detection and SuperGlue match, periodically providing feature matches for the prediction channel. The prediction channel synchronizes and fuses these guided feature matches into a matches club. The matches in the club could fastly predict over the image stream to provide the feature matches in frame rate, which is independent of the guidance channel. To accommodate our frame-rate feature association, we design a relative pose estimator based on MSCKF[29] in the back-end. The local odometry estimates from two UAVs are fused with the measurements of common features for the estimation. We conclude the main contributions of this work as follows:

  • •

    A dual-channel feature association algorithm between multiple UAVs is proposed in the front-end. Compared to the single-channel mode with SuperPoint and SuperGlue, the guidance and prediction channels decouple the time-consuming feature detection and match from the high-rate image stream. The novel design of periodic guidance and fast prediction effectively utilize each frame in the image stream, achieving a frame-rate feature association on the resource-constrained onboard computer. Extensive real-world experiments validate the superiority of the proposed method on real-time performance compared to the existing approaches.

  • •

    To accommodate the proposed dual-channel algorithm, an MSCKF-based multi-UAV relative pose estimator is developed in the back-end by fusing local odometry with the measurements of common features. Based on the MSCKF architecture, the relative state initialization, propagation, update, and landmark initialization are redesigned for the relative pose estimation. Further experiments validate that the proposed high-rate feature association enhances the real-time performance of the back-end estimator. The convergence speed of the back-end estimator is also improved compared to the existing methods.

This paper is structured as follows: In Section II, we introduce the dual-channel feature association in the front-end. In Section III, we present the MSCKF-based relative pose estimator in the back-end. The experiments are demonstrated in Section IV and the conclusion is finished in Section V.

II Dual-channel Feature Association

II-A Method Setup

We aim to provide a feature association method with distributed computing architecture across multiple UAVs. We take UAV i𝑖iitalic_i and UAV j𝑗jitalic_j to illustrate the cross-camera feature association algorithm. The raw images are processed in the local onboard computer to extract SuperPoint features individually. The UAV j𝑗jitalic_j continuously detects and broadcasts the SuperPoint key points to UAV i𝑖iitalic_i. The UAV i𝑖iitalic_i leverages the locally detected SuperPoint key points with the received key points to perform SuperGlue matching. The above process belongs to the guidance channel, aiming to provide high-quality feature matches for the prediction channel. The prediction channel is fully executed on UAV i𝑖iitalic_i. The received guided matches are firstly synchronized and stored in the matches club. When the new images arrive, the matches in the club are fastly predicted by Lucas-Kanade optical flow (LK-flow) to the current timestamp. The LK-flow has the advantages of tracking features on consecutive image streams and fast computation speed with GPU acceleration. We also establish an image caching database ImgDB on UAV i𝑖iitalic_i to collect newly captured images from two UAVs and make pairs to serve for the LK-flow. In the following discussion, the ImgDB also maintains the latest m𝑚mitalic_m pairs of images to address the asynchronous issue between the guidance channel and the prediction channel. IV Experiment and Discussion

IV-A Experiment Setup

The system architecture is shown in Fig.7. In the onboard sensors part, Xsens-Mti-630 is chosen as IMU, which provides angular velocity and acceleration for VIO propagation. A stereo Intel Realsense D455 camera is placed on the front-top of the UAV to provide the image for VIO and common feature detection. The VIO part adopts OpenVINS[31] to estimate the local odometry of the UAV. In the front-end, we distribute the computation among UAVs. Each UAV performs feature detection independently and broadcasts the results to other UAVs. One of the UAVs is selected to perform feature matching and combine the prediction channel to obtain feature matches in real-time. Each UAV is equipped with a TP-Link WDR7650 mesh router, which supports high-bandwidth and low-latency data exchange. The exchanged data includes the current VIO pose, image frame (for making image pairs), and extracted features. In our setup, the image stream is 640 ×\times× 480 at 30 Hz and the SuperPoint is extracted with 200 key points at 13 Hz. Thus, the total bandwidth of exchanged data is around 3.8 MB/s, including the compressed image 100 KB ×\times× 30 Hz, 200 key points with the feature ID, pixel location and SuperPoint descriptor 200 ×\times× (1+8+256) B ×\times× 13 Hz, the local odometry pose 28 B ×\times× 30 Hz. Through our test, the mesh router could provide up to 20 MB/s bandwidth with below 10 ms delay, which is adequate for the data exchange. Finally, the back-end is deployed on UAV i𝑖iitalic_i to estimate the relative pose with the VIO prediction and update from feature matches. The back-end on UAV j𝑗jitalic_j is idle, which supports UAV j𝑗jitalic_j to estimate relative pose with other UAVs. All programs are developed in the Linux Ubuntu 20.04 system and coded by C++14. The noetic version of Robot Operating System (ROS) is utilized to communicate among image processing, state fusion, and feedback control nodes. Especially, OpenCV 4.6.0 with CUDA 11.4 is used for image processing. The flight controller adopts PX4 Autopilot with v1.11.3 firmware. The feedback controller of UAV j𝑗jitalic_j adopts cascade Proportional Integral Derivative (PID) controller with position-velocity-attitude three loops. The integral part of the position loop will resist the effect of wind on position. The disturbance observer of our previous work[32] will also be deployed to further improve the robustness of the controller.

We select NVIDIA Xavier NX (8GB) as the onboard computer, which is widely adopted in UAV swarm[13]. Four typical experimental scenarios, including indoor and outdoor environments, are selected for evaluation as shown in Fig.8. Specifically, Fig.8 (a) presents the two UAVs in the flight field, where UAV j𝑗jitalic_j flies an 8-size trajectory autonomously. The two UAVs will be switched to manual flight mode if there are any potential collisions or unexpected behaviors. Fig.8 (b) shows two UAVs flying in the factory. Fig.8 (c) shows the outdoor experiments where two UAVs fly facing the facades of the buildings. Fig.8 (d) constructs a rendezvous scenario where two UAVs start from different rooms at the beginning and meet each other in a laboratory room. Due to the complex environments in Fig.8(b)(c)(d), the UAVs are manually manipulated to guarantee flight safety. The average speed of the UAV is 1.3 m/s (max 1.5 m/s and min 1.0 m/s) in four scenarios.

IV-B Run-time Analysis

We compare the run-time of the proposed dual-channel method with other popular feature association methods, including the original SuperPoint with SuperGlue (called SS), ORB with NN match and RANSAC filter (called ORB_NR), and SURF with NN match and RANSAC filter (called SURF_NR). In our proposed dual-channel structure, the guidance channel is based on SuperPoint and SuperGlue, and the prediction channel is accelerated by LK-flow. Therefore, our method is also called SSF. In the software configurations, we deploy CUDA 11.4 together with TensorRT 8.4 to accelerate the network inference of SuperPoint and SuperGlue. The LK-flow is implemented by OpenCV with CUDA acceleration. The Realsense D455 provides each UAV with a real-time color image with 640 ×\times× 480 pixels at 30 Hz. Since the run-time varies with the number of detected features, we list run-time statistics with different numbers of key points per image for each method, including 50, 200, and 500 key points. The larger number of key points takes more detection and matching time. We make average statistics on the above four scenarios for each method. In the two-UAV system, UAV j𝑗jitalic_j performs feature detection and broadcasts to UAV i𝑖iitalic_i. The UAV i𝑖iitalic_i performs local feature detection and feature match. Thus the run-time of feature association depends on UAV i𝑖iitalic_i. For the single-channel SS, SURF_NR and ORB_NR approaches, the run-time is constrained by one feature detection time and one feature match time. For the dual-channel SSF approach, the run-time of feature association is no longer constrained by feature detection and match in the guidance channel. The rate depends on the run-time of the prediction channel. The run-time statistics for feature association with four approaches are plotted in Fig.9.

For the SS, SURF_NR and ORB_NR approaches, the detailed run-time statistics are shown in Table I. As the number of feature points increases, the run-time of SS rises from 55 ms to 110 ms. The run-time of ORB_NR increases from 60 ms to 88 ms. The run-time of SURF_NR grows from 125 ms to 200 ms. Furthermore, we analyze the run-time along the sequence of image frames. A sequence containing 10 images is selected with 200 key points per image. The image frame rate is configured at 30 Hz (33 ms per image). From the results in Fig.10, we can see the processed image frames and the skipped image frames. The run-time of feature association in SS and ORB_NR is about 75 ms, which will impact the subsequent two frames of images. When the feature association of SS or ORB_NR completes, the two subsequent images are outdated and have to be skipped. The rate of feature association is constrained by waiting for the completion of feature detection and match. Thus, the real-time performance will degrade. The execution time of one SURF_NR makes five subsequent frames skipped, resulting in poorer real-time performance. In conclusion, the current pipeline of detection and match is tightly coupled with the image stream. The image frames in the high-rate image stream can not be fully utilized.

For the proposed dual-channel approach, the guidance channel adopts the pipeline of SS. The detailed run-time statistics are shown in Table II. We present the run-time analysis along the sequence of image frames in Fig.11. The run-time fluctuates along the image sequence, which reflects the different running stages. When new guided matches are generated from the guidance channel, the guided matches need to synchronize to the current moment first. We take the condition of kp200 for analysis. Due to the 74.3 ms taken in the guidance channel, the Fast-flow needs to perform two LK-flow computations with the outdated two frames. Then a match fusion is executed. Finally, the Normal-flow in the prediction channel performs one LK-flow computation to predict the fused matches from the last timestamp to the current timestamp. The total time is around 25.7 ms, including two LK-flow computations in Fast-flow, one match fusion, and one LK-flow computation in Normal-flow. At the subsequent frames after match fusion, the Normal-flow performs one LK-flow computation to predict the matches, which only costs 7.4 ms. The average run-time of SSF is 13.3 ms, which is 75 Hz. The run-time of Fast-flow depends on the number of outdated image frames. For the condition of 50 key points per image, the Fast-flow executes one LK-flow computation for the one outdated frame. For the condition of 500 key points per image, the Fast-flow executes three LK-flow computations for the three outdated frames. The limitation of the dual-channel structure lies in Fast-flow. If the image stream is set to 60 Hz, the ImgDB will stack two times outdated images for Fast-flow. Since each LK-flow will constantly cost about 7 ms, the whole process of Fast-flow can not meet the frame-rate requirement. Generally speaking, the 30 Hz image stream is adequate for most applications.

V Conclusion and Future Work

To achieve real-time relative pose estimation for UAVs by common features on the resource-constrained onboard computer, we present a dual-channel feature association algorithm to tackle the current non-real-time feature association issue. The parallel guidance and prediction channels decouple the time-consuming feature detection and match from the high-rate image stream. The periodic guidance and fast prediction effectively utilize each frame of the image stream to achieve a frame-rate feature association. In the back-end, an MSCKF-based relative pose estimator is developed by fusing the local odometry of two UAVs with the measurements of common features. Compared with existing approaches, the dual-channel improves the rate of feature association from 13 Hz to 30 Hz on NVIDIA NX 8G platform, compared with the single-channel SuperPoint and SuperGlue. The dual-channel front-end improves the update rate of visual observation and enhances the highly real-time relative pose estimation.

In the future, we will fuse the direct visual observation of neighboring robots in our previous work[35] with the common environmental feature approach in this work to achieve real-time and more accurate relative pose estimation. Based on the real-time feature association and relative state, we will try to construct the remote environments by collaborative triangulation and optimization. Our previous work[36] will be integrated to support fully autonomous flight and navigation in obstacle environments. In order to enable the system to work in extreme environments such as rains, waterproof materials will be added on UAVs for flight safety.
