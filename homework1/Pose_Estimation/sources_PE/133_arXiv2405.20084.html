Estimating Human Poses Across Datasets: A Unified Skeleton and Multi-Teacher Distillation Approach

Abstract

Human pose estimation is a key task in computer vision with various applications such as activity recognition and interactive systems. However, the lack of consistency in the annotated skeletons across different datasets poses challenges in developing universally applicable models. To address this challenge, we propose a novel approach integrating multi-teacher knowledge distillation with a unified skeleton representation. Our networks are jointly trained on the COCO and MPII datasets, containing 17 and 16 keypoints, respectively. We demonstrate enhanced adaptability by predicting an extended set of 21 keypoints, 4 (COCO) and 5 (MPII) more than original annotations, improving cross-dataset generalization. Our joint models achieved an average accuracy of 70.89 and 76.40, compared to 53.79 and 55.78 when trained on a single dataset and evaluated on both. Moreover, we also evaluate all 21 predicted points by our two models by reporting an AP of 66.84 and 72.75 on the Halpe dataset. This highlights the potential of our technique to address one of the most pressing challenges in pose estimation research and application - the inconsistency in skeletal annotations.

2 Related Work

The development of robust and accurate human pose estimation models and the use of knowledge distillation to enhance training efficiency represent two pivotal research areas in deep learning. This section reviews the current state of the art in these domains and highlights the gaps our work aims to bridge.

2.1 Human Pose Estimation

2D human pose estimation using deep learning is a fundamental task in computer vision to locate human joints (keypoints) in images or video frames. The two-stage techniques predominantly fall into either top-down or bottom-up categories. Top-down methodsÂ [Li etÂ al.(2021a)Li, Zhang, Wang, Yang, Yang, Xia, and Zhou, Xu etÂ al.(2022)Xu, Zhang, Zhang, and Tao, Khirodkar etÂ al.(2021)Khirodkar, Chari, Agrawal, and Tyagi, Jiang etÂ al.(2023)Jiang, Lu, Zhang, Ma, Han, Lyu, Li, and Chen] first detect individual people in the image and then predict keypoints for each detected person, often leading to higher accuracy but at the cost of computational efficiency. In contrast, bottom-up methodsÂ [Cheng etÂ al.(2020)Cheng, Xiao, Wang, Shi, Huang, and Zhang, Osokin(2018), Wang etÂ al.(2022)Wang, Li, Cai, Chen, and Han, Papandreou etÂ al.(2018)Papandreou, Zhu, Chen, Gidaris, Tompson, and Murphy] detect all keypoints in the image first and then cluster them into individual poses, which can be more scalable but might struggle with accuracy in dense human environments. Alternatively, some recent worksÂ [Nie etÂ al.(2019)Nie, Feng, Zhang, and Yan, Shi etÂ al.(2021)Shi, Wei, Yu, Tan, Ren, and Pu, Jin etÂ al.(2022)Jin, Xu, Wang, Xiao, Guo, Nie, and Zhao, Shi etÂ al.(2022)Shi, Wei, Li, Ren, and Tan, Lu etÂ al.(2023)Lu, Jiang, Li, Li, Chen, and Yang] use a one-stage approach using a single model for the complete pose estimation pipeline. Keypoint predictions are typically represented directly as coordinates or, more commonly, through heatmaps, where the latter involves predicting a confidence map for each keypoint and has shown considerable success due to its robustness to pixel-level noise and variability in human appearances. These methods leverage a variety of network architectures, with more recent works using transformersÂ [Xu etÂ al.(2022)Xu, Zhang, Zhang, and Tao, Li etÂ al.(2021a)Li, Zhang, Wang, Yang, Yang, Xia, and Zhou, Sun etÂ al.(2024)Sun, Gu, Wang, Yang, and Yao, Shi etÂ al.(2022)Shi, Wei, Li, Ren, and Tan, Zheng etÂ al.(2021)Zheng, Zhu, Mendieta, Yang, Chen, and Ding], which have been adapted better to capture the spatial hierarchies inherent in human poses. Despite significant advancements, the challenge remains to enhance the precision and efficiency of these models, especially in unconstrained real-world scenarios where occlusions and complex human interactions are common.

2.2 Knowledge Distillation

Knowledge distillationÂ [Hinton etÂ al.(2015)Hinton, Vinyals, and Dean] is a technique originally developed to compress the knowledge of a large, complex model (teacher) into a smaller, more efficient model (student) with minimal performance loss. Logit-based knowledge distillationÂ [Hinton etÂ al.(2015)Hinton, Vinyals, and Dean, Chen etÂ al.(2020)Chen, Mei, Wang, Feng, and Chen, Jin etÂ al.(2023)Jin, Wang, and Lin, Li etÂ al.(2020b)Li, Huang, Chen, Luo, Cai, and Pan, Mirzadeh etÂ al.(2019)Mirzadeh, Farajtabar, Li, Levine, Matsukawa, and Ghasemzadeh, Zhang etÂ al.(2023)Zhang, Shen, Liu, Liu, Bendersky, Najork, and Zhang, Zhao etÂ al.(2022)Zhao, Cui, Song, Qiu, and Liang, Zhao etÂ al.(2022)Zhao, Cui, Song, Qiu, and Liang] utilizes the soft output probabilities from the teacher model to guide the training of the student model. By employing a loss function that combines temperature-scaled cross-entropy with Kullback-Leibler divergenceÂ [Kullback and Leibler(1951)], the student model is encouraged not only to mimic the teacherâ€™s output distribution but also to achieve correct label predictions on its own. This method effectively transfers the intricate decision boundaries learned by the teacher to the student, enhancing its performance beyond what could be achieved through direct training alone. Feature-based distillationÂ [Ahn etÂ al.(2019)Ahn, Hu, Damianou, Lawrence, and Dai, Chen etÂ al.(2022)Chen, Mei, Zhang, Wang, Feng, and Chen, Chen etÂ al.(2021)Chen, Liu, Zhao, and Jia, Guo etÂ al.(2023)Guo, Yan, Li, and Lin, Heo etÂ al.(2019)Heo, Kim, Yun, Park, Kwak, and Choi, Li etÂ al.(2021b)Li, Ye, Song, Huang, and Pan, Lin etÂ al.(2022)Lin, Xie, Wang, Yu, Chang, Liang, and Wang, Liu etÂ al.(2019)Liu, Chen, Liu, Qin, Luo, and Wang, Romero etÂ al.(2015)Romero, Ballas, Kahou, Chassang, Gatta, and Bengio, Tian etÂ al.(2020)Tian, Krishnan, and Isola, Yim etÂ al.(2017)Yim, Joo, Bae, and Kim] and relation-based distillaionÂ [Huang etÂ al.(2022)Huang, You, Wang, Qian, and Xu, Li etÂ al.(2022)Li, Li, Wang, Zhang, Wu, and Liang, Park etÂ al.(2019)Park, Kim, Lu, and Cho, Peng etÂ al.(2019)Peng, Jin, Liu, Li, Wu, Liu, Zhou, and Zhang, Tung and Mori(2019), Yang etÂ al.(2021)Yang, MartÃ­nez, Bulat, and Tzimiropoulos] have also been proposed to improve the knowledge transfer between teacher and student. Recent advancements, such as the introduction of a teacher assistant modelÂ [Mirzadeh etÂ al.(2020)Mirzadeh, Farajtabar, Li, Levine, Matsukawa, and Ghasemzadeh], have further refined this process by staging the knowledge transfer through an intermediate-sized model, which bridges the gap between the high-capacity teacher and the smaller student. Knowledge distillation has also been used to enhance the performance of human pose estimation methodsÂ [Zhao etÂ al.(2021)Zhao, Gao, Ge, and Tian, Thai etÂ al.(2023)Thai, Nham, Tran, Bui, Ninh, and Tran, Yang etÂ al.(2023)Yang, Zeng, Yuan, and Li, Li etÂ al.(2021c)Li, Ye, Song, Huang, and Pan], including some worksÂ [Thai etÂ al.(2023)Thai, Nham, Tran, Bui, Ninh, and Tran, Zhao etÂ al.(2021)Zhao, Gao, Ge, and Tian] that use multiple teacher networks.

2.3 Cross-Dataset Human Pose Estimation

Cross-dataset adaption has recently gained attention for 3D pose estimationÂ [Kundu etÂ al.(2020)Kundu, Patravali, RADHAKRISHNAN, etÂ al., Gholami etÂ al.(2022)Gholami, Wandt, Rhodin, Ward, and Wang, Guan etÂ al.(2021)Guan, Xu, Wang, Ni, and Yang, Zhang etÂ al.(2020)Zhang, Nie, and Feng]. To achieve this adaption, some of these methods use generative networksÂ [Nie etÂ al.(2023)Nie, Fang, Cheng, Lin, and Li], mesh reconstructionÂ [Guan etÂ al.(2021)Guan, Xu, Wang, Ni, and Yang], or unsupervised learning techniquesÂ [Kundu etÂ al.(2020)Kundu, Patravali, RADHAKRISHNAN, etÂ al.]. In 2D human pose estimation, cross-dataset keypoint prediction remains scarce. Some methods available in MMPoseÂ [Contributors(2020)], including RTMPoseÂ [Jiang etÂ al.(2023)Jiang, Lu, Zhang, Ma, Han, Lyu, Li, and Chen] and RTMOÂ [Lu etÂ al.(2023)Lu, Jiang, Li, Li, Chen, and Yang] are trained on a "cocktail" of multiple datasets called Body7 and Body8. However, they are often only trained to predict a subset of these points instead of predicting a union of all available annotations across the datasets.

4 Experiments

This section details the experiments we conducted to validate the effectiveness of our proposed approach, including a description of the baselines, experimental setup, and the results. We also show examples of extended annotations we created for the COCO and MPII images.

4.1 Baselines

In our experiments, we use various RTMPose modelsÂ [Jiang etÂ al.(2023)Jiang, Lu, Zhang, Ma, Han, Lyu, Li, and Chen] trained on the COCO Keypoints dataset and the MPII Human Pose dataset. We compare the networks trained with our proposed approach with their counterparts trained on individual datasets. Specifically, we report results with RTMPose-t and RTMPose-s trained on each dataset separately and then compare the same models when trained on a combined dataset using our conditional loss and multi-teacher distillation. To distill the student networks, we use pre-trained RTMPose-m models as teachers.

4.2 Experimental Setup

We used the pre-trained weights of RTMPose-m teachers from MMPose for both the COCO and MPII datasets. We performed various experiments where the studentsâ€“RTMPose-s and RTMPose-tâ€“were trained from scratch for 420 epochs. The training used the AdamWÂ [Loshchilov and Hutter(2017)] optimizer with an initial learning rate of 4â¢eâˆ’34superscripte34\mathrm{e}^{-3}4 roman_e start_POSTSUPERSCRIPT - 3 end_POSTSUPERSCRIPT. 4.3 Evaluation Metrics

In our experimental analysis, we utilize two commonly accepted metrics for evaluating human pose estimation models: Percentage of Correct Keypoints (PCK) and Average Precision (AP). PCK measures the accuracy of keypoint predictions by determining the percentage of predicted keypoints that fall within a specified pixel distance from the true keypoints, effectively capturing the precision of spatial localization. AP, on the other hand, provides a comprehensive measure of precision and recall across various thresholds, assessing the modelâ€™s ability to identify keypoints while penalizing false positives correctly. These metrics are instrumental in benchmarking the performance of our pose estimation models across different datasets, highlighting their precision in keypoint detection and overall detection reliability.
