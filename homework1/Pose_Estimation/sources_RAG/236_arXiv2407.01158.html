Abstract

Interactions with billion-scale large language models typically yield long-form responses due to their extensive parametric capacities, along with retrieval-augmented features. While detailed responses provide insightful viewpoint of a specific subject, they frequently generate redundant and less engaging content that does not meet user interests. In this work, we focus on the role of query outlining (i.e., selected sequence of queries) in scenarios that users request a specific range of information, namely coverage-conditioned (C2) scenarios. For simulating C2 scenarios, we construct QTree, 10K sets of information-seeking queries decomposed with various perspectives on certain topics. By utilizing QTree, we train QPlanner, a 7B language model generating customized query outlines that follow coverage-conditioned queries. We analyze the effectiveness of generated outlines through automatic and human evaluation, targeting on retrieval-augmented generation (RAG). Moreover, the experimental results demonstrate that QPlanner with alignment training can further provide outlines satisfying diverse user interests. Our resources are available at https://github.com/youngerous/qtree.

1 Introduction

Curiosity is attributed to perceptual knowledge and sequence of interestsÂ (BERLYNE, 1954). The emerging performance of recent large language models (LLMs) enables providing long and detailed responses by utilizing their deep parametric knowledge. Meanwhile, human-machine interaction interface (e.g., chat and acoustic interface) that has been studied for a long timeÂ (Levin etÂ al., 2000) allows users to request customized information in the form of instructions. More recent proprietary chat interface services such as ChatGPTÂ (OpenAI, 2023), GeminiÂ (Google, 2024), and BingChat are accelerating the exploration of human curiosity.

LLMsâ€™ long responses consist of innumerable combinations of relevant topics, as there is no gold standard in long-form textÂ (Krishna etÂ al., 2021; Xu etÂ al., 2022, 2023). However, LLMs often fail to provide tailored responses when usersâ€™ queries get complicated. For instance, a user can request LLMs to provide information on a topic, Generative AI, especially focusing on historical context without describing recent trends (since the user already understands the latest news), but it is too complicated for LLMs to generate meticulously-composed responses. Since humans have their own background knowledge and curiosity level, we define these queries that constrain the information coverage of certain topics as coverage-conditioned (C2) queries. Specifically, our scenario conceptualizes â€œcoverageâ€ by assuming that users can request LLMs to include or exclude a specific subtopic within long-form responses.

There have been several studies to generate responses following usersâ€™ instructionsÂ (Ouyang etÂ al., 2022; Wang etÂ al., 2022; Wei etÂ al., 2022a; Taori etÂ al., 2023; Asai etÂ al., 2023). Among these trends, we focus on query outlining, which is a sequence of queries representing intermediate subtopics within a long text, demonstrating its effectiveness in long-form story generationÂ (Fan etÂ al., 2018; Sun etÂ al., 2022; Yang etÂ al., 2022, 2023; Wang etÂ al., 2024; Shao etÂ al., 2024; Lee etÂ al., 2024). On top of that, we further raise a question: â€œHow to generate customized outlines for long-form responses in the retrieval-augmented setup?â€ Well-generated outlines should align with user perspectivesÂ (Shao etÂ al., 2024) and show improved performance in downstream tasks, such as document retrieval and response generation. In our work, outlines consisting of relevant topics to C2 queries are expected to be utilized both as better search queries and intermediate content drafts, thereby improving the end-to-end retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) performance.

To explore the way to generate customized outlines, we propose QTree, 10K hierarchical sets of information-seeking queries interpreting user queries of existing datasets with diverse perspectives. Hierarchies of QTree are comprised based on the abstraction level of the main topic. For example, as illustrated in Figure 1, hierarchical topics related to processes after pretraining (i.e., Finetuning and RLHF) are selected among various viewpoints in LLMs. We assume that the selected subset of QTree performs a role as an outline for document search and response generation.

Specifically, QPlanner generates outlines to arbitrary C2 queries by learning the outline-extracting capabilities of LLMs. We evaluate the performance of QPlanner by measuring whether generated outlines follow C2 queries, both with automatic and human evaluation. Moreover, we verify that training QPlanner with preference alignment can further improve the outline quality and end-to-end RAG output. Our contributions are summarized as follows:

  • â€¢

2 Related Work

2.1 Query Modification with LLMs

Integrating retrieval systems with LLMs has become crucial in enhancing retrieval-augmented systems, where query reformulation plays a pivotal role in improving information retrieval outcomes. Recent advancements involve prompting LLMs to supply detailed information, such as expected documents or pseudo-answers, for query expansionÂ (Wang etÂ al., 2023; Jagerman etÂ al., 2023). Additionally, thereâ€™s a growing interest in utilizing reward signals to fine-tune the query reformulation model, further optimizing search results based on the ranking of retrieved documentsÂ (Ma etÂ al., 2023; Yoon etÂ al., 2024). Recent efforts have also focused on breaking down complex questions into simpler subqueries, leveraging the decomposition for more accurate retrieval and response generationÂ (Khot etÂ al., 2023). Additionally, a line of studies (Yao etÂ al., 2023; Press etÂ al., 2023; Khattab etÂ al., 2023) merge the concept of Chain-of-Thoughts (CoT)Â (Wei etÂ al., 2022b) with interaction through retrieval systems. Furthermore, most recent studiesÂ (Oh etÂ al., 2024; Weller etÂ al., 2024) introduce the inclusion of instructional scenarios in the information-seeking process.

While having similar query modification strategies (e.g., query decomposition or instructional scenarios), we consider query outlining as an effective strategy before generating long-form responses. Previously, a hierarchical structure has shown remarkable performance on story generation taskÂ (Fan etÂ al., 2018). Yang etÂ al. (2022, 2023) have also emphasized the importance of outline for structured story generation. More recently, Shao etÂ al. (2024) have explored the role of the outline as a medium to present diverse perspectives and constructed outlines by iterative conversational processes. Furthermore, Lee etÂ al. (2024) have improved the quality of free-form writing by outline augmentation. Above these studies, we position our work as the automatic exploration of a better outline for retrieval-augmented long-form generation in C2 scenarios.

2.2 Evaluation of Long-form Responses

Evaluating long-form responses of LLMs presents a significant challenge due to the subjective and complex nature of such assessments. Recent researchÂ (Krishna etÂ al., 2021; Xu etÂ al., 2022, 2023) highlight the limitations of current automated metrics in accurately assessing long-form texts, underscoring the necessity for detailed, fine-grained evaluation methods. Some holistic approaches consider a wide array of model capabilities, introducing multi-metric evaluation settingsÂ (Liang etÂ al., 2023; Gehrmann etÂ al., 2023; Shevlane etÂ al., 2023; Ye etÂ al., 2024). Additionally, the push towards fine-grained atomic evaluation settings for tasks like fact verification and summarization indicates a growing interest in detailed assessmentsÂ (Min etÂ al., 2023; Krishna etÂ al., 2023). Recent research has also explored model-based evaluation, where learned models determine automated scoresÂ (Yuan etÂ al., 2021; Liu etÂ al., 2023; Kim etÂ al., 2024). While these studies consider long-form responses themselves as the target to evaluate, we evaluate both outlines and responses by following insights from Kellogg (1988) where outlines alleviate the cognitive overload of reading long text (i.e., serving as an effective content draft) while representing the core structure of long-form writing.

3.4.2 Evaluating Outline Quality

In order to evaluate the quality of candidate outlines, we leverage LLM (gpt-4-0125-preview) to serve as a judge deciding whether the content on each candidate outline follows C2 query. More precisely, we prompt the model to assign scores, measuring how faithfully the outline aligns with the C2 query. The scoring mechanism employs a five-point Likert scale, along with a corresponding rationale generation for the evaluation. Since outlines have significantly shorter context than long-form text while containing core contentsÂ (Kellogg, 1988), it is expected that the evaluation of the LLM shows better alignment with human intuition than directly evaluating long context.

3.5 Training QPlanner (Step 3)

To deal with arbitrary C2 queries and generate corresponding outlines, we train a language model named QPlanner. We instruct QPlanner to sequentially generate QTree and selected outline, as we intend that QTree serves like an intermediate Chain-of-ThoughtÂ (Wei etÂ al., 2022b) reasoning process.

In the training phase, we employ supervised fine-tuning (SFT) and alignment tuning for the training QPlanner. First, we train the Llama-2-7B-Chat modelÂ (Touvron etÂ al., 2023) using 10K C2 queries mapped with 31K candidate outline pairs, which are constructed through SectionÂ 3.4.1. This training phase allows the model to generate formatted outlines following C2 queries. Then we further align the preferred outline by adopting a variant of direct preference optimization (DPO)Â (Rafailov etÂ al., 2023). Following Tunstall etÂ al. (2023) that show the possibility of distilling the preference of large teacher models into the targeted model, we utilize LLM evaluation previously acquired in SectionÂ 3.4.2 as a reward signal for aligning QPlanner. We regard the chosen sample as the highest-scored and the rejected sample as the lowest-scored outline. We do not use samples whose highest and lowest scores are the same in the alignment stage. The amount of the final training sample is 31,488 in SFT and 8,568 in DPO, respectively. Refer to AppendixÂ F for further details, such as hyperparameters.

4.2.2 Response Evaluation

In addition to outline evaluation, we recruit another participants to evaluate 100 randomly sampled RAG responses. Ten evaluators are assigned for each response. According toÂ Kim etÂ al. (2023), the writing format of model responses affects human preferences. Therefore, we fix the format with Markdown to compare responses by focusing only on their content. In addition, we prompt LLM to generate responses by strictly relying on given evidence to prevent LLM from arbitrarily responding with its parametric knowledge. We provide a description of the document retrieval procedure in SectionÂ G.2.1.

Regarding evaluation criteria, we first instruct participants to judge whether generated responses follow requirements within C2 queries or not (Query Satisfaction in FigureÂ 4). We guide to annotate "Yes" if responses at least partially handle topics within C2 queries. For response pairs annotated as "Yes" in both models, participants select their preferred response from the two models (Response Preference in FigureÂ 4).

QPlanner as Better Search Query

We verify whether queries within outlines can help search relevant documents. We compare responses of vanilla RAG with those of QPlanner-DPO using the exactly same prompt. In other words, outline queries of QPlanner-DPO only affect search results. As illustrated in FigureÂ 4(a), we find that conventional RAG pipeline does not properly handle C2 queries, whose requirements are far more complicated than normal queries. Furthermore, among responses that satisfy the requirements of C2 queries, responses of QPlanner-DPO are mostly preferred.

We further explore whether better outlines lead to better responses. In this setup, we compare responses of QPlanner-SFT with those of QPlanner-DPO. Likewise, the exactly same prompt is used for the comparison and queries within outlines are included to compose responses, along with document searching. Results in FigureÂ 4(b) indicate that QPlanner aligned with preference outlines tends to provide preferred outlines, although QPlanner-SFT also shows a similar tendency with QPlanner-DPO, which is different from that of Vanilla-RAG.

For both studies, we conduct two McNemarâ€™s testsÂ (McNemar, 1947) to examine whether the differences we found are statistically significant. 5 Conclusion

Customizing information coverage within C2 queries requires complex selection of subtopics to compose long-form responses. We investigate the role of query outline in terms of assistive search queries and intermediate content draft for RAG task. To simulate C2 scenarios, we construct QTree, hierarchical sets of queries representing diverse perspectives of the original query. Subsequently, we train QPlanner which extracts customized outlines from QTree for C2 queries. Our findings based on automatic and human evaluation show that (1) preference-aligned QPlanner can provide better outlines, (2) outlines enable searching more relevant documents, and (3) well-generated outlines lead to preferred responses. We believe our work shows the possibility of QTree as a testbed for exploring effective pre-writing strategy to deal with complicated queries.

Limitation

We fix the number of queries in each outline to four, but it can be adjusted (i.e., composing more or less queries) according to the topic. We plan to adapt outlines in a flexible manner in our future work.

It should be noted that retrieved documents in our RAG setup can also affect final responses. Although we set the same searching configuration among all methodologies, additional fact verification is still needed for practical applications.

In addition, despite demonstrating significant performance gain both in automatic and human evaluation, we find that state-of-the-art LLM still has difficulty in generating long-form responses that handle detailed coverage of C2 query. This is presumably due to the complexness of C2 queries, but it arises the importance of constructing meticulous benchmark evaluating long-form responses of complicated queries. This will be another direction of our future work.
